### Slide 13


Variations in Sensory Ability
Hyposmia: reduced sensitivity to one or more odors
Anosmia: general inability to smell
Specific Anosmia: inability to smell certain compounds or families of compounds, but all other smell functions are normal
Source: Serby and Chobor (1992), Lawless (2010)


### Slide 14


Variations in Sensory Ability
Hyperosmia: increased sensitivity to one or more odors
Dysosmia: distorted sense of smell
Phantosmia: perception of bad odor when there is no actual bad odor
Parosmia: atypical perception of a specific odor stimulus
Source: Serby and Chobor (1992)


### Slide 15


Variations in Sensory Ability
Each person has different smell abilities
“Different smelling equipment”
Not everyone will always agree on the aroma profile
A specific anosmia is not a disqualifier from a sensory panel unless it is that particular smell being evaluated
Source: Lawless (2010)


### Slide 16


Variations in Sensory Ability
Taste sensitivity varies among individuals
Bitter compounds have particularly varied results
Some people have genetically inherited insensitivities to certain bitter compounds
PTC, PROP, quinine and other compounds are used to test this
1/3 of Caucasian people cannot taste PTC or PROP at the concentration detected by most people
Source: Lawless (2010)


### Slide 17


Variations in Sensory Ability
Supertasters
Hypersensitive group of individuals
Number of papillae and taste buds results in increased responsiveness
Increased ability in one taste (e.g., bitterness) does not necessarily mean an increased taste ability in others (e.g., sourness, sweetness)
Source: Lawless (2010)

### Slide 55


Visual Attributes - Legs
Wait… what about legs?
Also called “tears” or “cathedral”
Water and ethanol on the side of the class exhibit surface tension properties
As ethanol evaporates, water continues to climb up the side of the glass, the heavy water droplets fall and form tears
Used to be used as a sign of quality, but modern-day wine evaluations do not put much emphasis on legs, as many things can affect the formation:
Ethanol content
Sugar content
Room temperature
Room humidity
Image source: wineguy.co.nz
Source: Gugliotti and Silverstein (2004)

### Slide 71


Taste Attributes: Sourness
Malic acid and resulting sourness comes from the fruit itself
Dependent on cultivar, climate, growing practices, etc.
Other acids important for cider flavor are derived from fermentation and/or spoilage:
Lactic acid
Acetic acid
Succinic acid


### Slide 72


Taste Attributes: Sourness
Lactic Acid
Main acid found in fermented dairy products
Produced by Lactic Acid Bacteria (LAB) during malolactic fermentation
LAB break down malic acid, reducing perceived acidity
Malolactic fermentation process may affect other sensory characteristics besides taste/sourness


### Slide 73


Taste Attributes: Sourness
Acetic Acid
Results from bacterial conversion of alcohol
The conversion increases the concentration of acid as alcohol is consumed
Important on an olfactory level
“Vinegar” aromatic effects important for olfactory perception


### Slide 74


Taste Attributes: Sourness
Succinic Acid
Byproduct of alcoholic fermentation
Present in very low concentrations
Sensory threshold in cider unknown, but water-based succinic acid solutions have yielded salty and bitter tastes
Source: Coulter et al. (2004)


### Slide 75


Taste Attributes: Sourness
Citric Acid
Found in relatively low concentrations in apples compared to other acids
Somewhat higher concentrations in pears, but still significantly less than malic
May be added, though not typically recommended due to potential for it to be converted to acetic acid by LAB

### Slide 120


Mouthfeel
Effects of maturation and aging
Polyphenols condense/polymerize to create softer, rounder mouthfeel
Acids may be consumed during MLF to decrease harshness (may be a fault if undesired)
Ethanol may be consumed to produce acetic acid (typically a fault)
Wooden maturation vessels may provide some additional tannins and oxygen exposure 🡪 further polymerization of tannins
Source: Clarke and Bakker (2005)

### Slide 126


Origins of Aroma Compounds
In order to reach olfactory bulb, compounds need to be in vapor phase
Equilibrium between volatile and non-volatile phase determined by the partition coefficient, Kj,a-w
Ratio of weight of the compound in the air vs water
The higher the value, the more the compound is in the vapor state
Dependent on:
Water solubility
Boiling point
Compounds with high boiling points but low solubility have high partition coefficients, while compounds with low boiling points and high water solubility have low values.
Source: Clarke and Bakker (2005)

### Slide 136


Origins of Cider Aromas: Esters
Types of Esters
1. Acetate esters
Formed by the reaction of acetyl-CoA and ethanol
Relatively small and able to diffuse more readily across cell membranes
Examples: ethyl acetate, isoamyl acetate, 2-methylbutyl acetate, 2-phenylethyl acetate
Image source: Hirst, M. and Richter C. 2016. Review of Aroma Formation through Metabolic Pathways of Saccharomyces cerevisiae in Beverage Fermentations. Am. J. Enol. Vitic. 67:4

### Slide 140


Origins of Cider Aromas: Esters
Yeast produce esters in concentrations near human sensory thresholds
Small fluctuations in ester production can make large sensory impacts on cider
Yeast genetics most influential in ester production due to varying levels of enzyme expression
Temperature, oxygen, and substrate availability also impact ester production but to a lesser degree
Source: Hirst and Richter (2016)


### Slide 141


Origins of Cider Aromas: Higher Alcohols
Higher (Fusel) Alcohols impart a variety of aromas ranging from alcohol, banana, rose/flower, and cidery aromas
Produced from amino acids that go through the Ehrlich pathway
Source: Hirst and Richter (2016), Dzalio (2017)


### Slide 142


Origins of Cider Aromas: Higher Alcohols
Ehrlich Pathway
Yeast modify amino acids in three steps
Transamination – amino group transferred to another amino acid
Decarboxylation – removal of the carboxyl group
Reduction or oxidation
Image source: nutrientsreview.com
Source: Hirst and Richter (2016), Dzalio (2017)

### Slide 146


Origins of Cider Aromas: Ketones
Ketones produce a range of aromas that include nutty, toasty, buttery, and rancid
Produced during the synthesis pathways of amino acids valine and isoleucine
Source: Hirst and Richter (2016), Dzalio (2017)


### Slide 147


Origins of Cider Aromas: Ketones
Most relevant ketone in ciders and wine is diacetyl
Produced if valine synthesis pathway is inefficient and precursors to diacetyl build up
Source: Hirst and Richter (2016), Dzalio (2017)


### Slide 148


Origins of Cider Aromas: Ketones
Very high concentrations of diacetyl may indicate bacterial spoilage problems during MLF
Diacetyl production also dependent on yeast genetics and environmental factors:
Increased pH increases the production of diacetyl
Increased temperature, fermentation time, supplementation with valine, and sugar concentration decreases the production of diacetyl
Source: Hirst and Richter (2016), Dzalio (2017)

### Slide 153


Origins of Cider Aromas: Sulfur Compounds
Sulfur-containing compounds produce aromas such as rotten eggs and cooked cabbage

### Slide 154


Origins of Cider Aromas: Sulfur Compounds
Insufficient nitrogen during fermentation causes a buildup of sulfide in the cell
Sulfide is converted to hydrogen sulfide
Hydrogen sulfide is exported out of the cell

Source: Hirst and Richter (2016), Dzalio (2017)
