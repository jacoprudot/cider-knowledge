C. MAKING CIDER & PERRY – STAGE ONE: FRUIT SELECTION TO MATURATION
Fruit Selection for Cider & Perry Production – Key Considerations
Cider:
ü	Most apple varieties are suitable for making cider!
ü	A blend of apples tends to work best (3 – 4 varieties processed together). A reasonable level of acidity is required 
(important for pH control), but not too much. Aromatic apples can lead to more complex flavours in the ciders 
being made but it is important to note that most of the aroma components found in cider will be derived from the 
metabolic activity of yeasts (& bacteria) and will not carry-over from the original fruit. 
ü	For tannin (bitterness, astringency, a greater ‘body’ in the cider and more aroma complexity) then cider apples 
should be used. Although crab apples (& Quince!) can be used, care should be taken not to use too many as harsh 
flavours can be produced in the final cider. 
Perry:
ü	It can be difficult to make good perry with dessert pears as some varieties contain aroma compounds that are 
unpleasant in the fermented perry. Often better results can be obtained using dessert pear juice concentrate, as the 
concentration process removes these undesirable components.
ü	With Perry pears, however, potential white wine-like qualities can be achieved.
Harvesting
As introduced above, it is important to harvest apples at the correct stage of ripeness. If apples are unripe:
•	 The sugar content will be low
•	 They typically contain high starch levels
•	 The flavour will not be fully developed
•	 (The acid levels will be high)
When to Harvest Apples
1. Fruit can be readily shaken from trees
2. Pips (seeds) brown
3. Waxy skin
4. Use starch test: Immerse a cut apple in an iodine solution.  If unripe, starch content evenly spread across whole  
surface. As the apple matures, the starch disappears from the central portion, leaving only a small amount on the 
periphery.
5. Measure sugar levels
Fruit Storage
Many varieties of cider apples, if harvested carefully with little or no bruising, will benefit from a period of storage prior 
to processing. Potential benefits include an increase in Specific Gravity (SG) and improvements in flavour as a result of 
oxidation of the apple phenolics:
 Breakwells seedling: Maximum 3 weeks
 T remlett’s Bitter: Maximum 3 weeks
 Dabinett: 3-4 weeks
 Yarlington Mill: 3-4 weeks
 Michelin: 3-4 weeks
 Kingston Black: 3-4 weeks
It is recognized, however, that this is not often practicable particularly on a large commercial scale. Care is also needed 
with some fruit as it can get difficult to press if over ripe.

Fruit Quality
The size and shape of the fruit and presence of skin blemishes or surface cracking on apples and pears for cider/perry 
production does not cause any problems. Bruising itself is also not a problem, but can rapidly lead to rotting. However 
the use of rotten fruit should be avoided as far as is possible, as this will be a significant source of unwanted microbial 
contamination, it will reduce the effectiveness of any sulphur dioxide being used (see below) and will make the fruit more 
difficult to press. 
It should be noted, based on evidence to date, potential food pathogens (e.g. E. coli) do not survive the fermentation 
process and the micro-toxin patulin that can also be found on rotten apples and is an established and regulated potential 
contaminant of apple juice, is metabolized during cider making by the fermentation yeast to the less toxic ascladiol.
Milling & Pressing
After delivery to the Cider Mill, the first stage of the cider making process is to mill the fruit into a fine pulp (“mash”) 
prior to juice extraction in a press. Most fruit, however, unless it has been handpicked and is completely clean, will benefit 
from washing prior to processing. Water-based handling systems will also provide a mechanism for fruit transport and 
trapping stones - apples float and stones sink! Unfortunately, pears also sink, so perry producers will modify fruit handling 
systems accordingly.
Mills should be matched to the capacity of the press and, ideally, should be adjustable in order to produce varying sizes 
of mash particles as determined by the type of fruit being processed. T ypically, with high tannin cider apples, the fruit can 
be ground very finely, however with high pectin dessert fruit, better juice yields will be obtained when the apples or pears 
are more coarsely ground.
There are a number of different types of cider presses in current use. T ypical juice yields are between 650 to 850 liters per 
tonne, dependent on the variety of fruit processed (e.g. cider apples tend to have higher yields than dessert apples) and 
the type of press being used. After juice extraction, the solid residue left is called “pomace”, usually sent to farmers for use 
in feeding cattle and pigs, or used in biomass energy systems. 
The main types of presses are as follows:
1. Batch Presses (Pressure)
a) Bed press / hydraulic ram press / pack press / rack and cloth press
“Cheeses” are built up between slatted racks, which are configured in a vertical stack (e.g. Voran pack press) or 
horizontally (e.g. Goodnature presses)
 High juice yields, but labor intensive. Typically used by smaller-scale producers.
b) Horizontal cage presses e.g. Bucher HP
The press is repeatedly filled and pressed until press chamber is 80% full.  The outer casing and piston are 
withdrawn and the cylinder rotated to discharge pomace.
During filling, hydraulics are turned off, piston pushed back by the mash being pumped in. During pressing, the juice 
will flow through sleeved channels.
High juice yields, high capital cost, high power requirements, fully automated. Used by many of the large-scale producers.

2. Continuous Presses (Pressure)
a) Belt (band) presses e.g. Voran
The mash is squeezed between continuous belts (either between rollers or by belt tension). The juice flows  
through the perforated bottom belt and pomace removed by scraper / auger.
Lower juice yields than hydraulic presses and are typically more difficult to keep clean, but are much cheaper than 
horizontal cage presses. These types of press are becoming increasingly popular with medium-scale producers.
Other fruit processing treatments
1. Use of press-aids: Addition of inert cellulose-based materials (e.g. rice hulls) after milling and prior to pressing can 
improve juice extraction particularly with ripe dessert pears or high-pectin dessert apples.
2. Hot water diffusion: The pressed pomace is broken up and washed with hot water to extract residual phenolics  
and sugars.
3. Enzymes: If required, pectinase can be added to the mash and/or pressed juice to remove pectin. Amylase can also 
be added to the juice if residual starch is present
Juice T reatment
After the juice is extracted, samples are commonly sent to the on-site laboratory for analysis. Cider producers, both 
large and small, should have equipment to undertake certain basic tests. The fresh-pressed juice should be checked for 
its sugar concentration (usually indirectly by measuring its Specific Gravity (S.G.)), pH and the concentration of acid. 
The adjustment of pH to a defined level (possibly by adding food-grade acid to a pH below 3.8), is key to ensuring that a 
successful and efficient fermentation is achieved without spoilage and the production of off-flavours by unwanted micro-
organisms. The addition of sulphur dioxide to fresh-pressed juice is also typically carried out (see below).
Apple & Pear Juice Concentrate
The cider industry uses three main types of concentrate:
•	 Dessert / Culinary apple at between 15 – 40gl-1 acidity
•	 Bittersweet apple at between 15 - 25gl-1 acidity
•	 Pear 
If concentrate is to be used, then the following points should be considered:
o It should be purchased from a reputable supplier. There is, unfortunately, a lot of badly made (and cheap!) 
concentrate available, but a well-made concentrate can be fermented to a very good cider.
o Ideally, store at 40 – 100C for a maximum of 12 months. Keep the container sealed to avoid water condensing on 
the surface. 
o A diluted sample should be assessed:
o Meets specification (as required) e.g. usually at 700 Brix / SG 1.350
o The taste & flavour should be acceptable i.e. apple/pear-like, not burnt or caramelized and with fermented or 
other off-flavours.
o T ypically, it should be diluted with potable water to “standard” juice strength prior to use (to SG 1.045; a 6-fold 
dilution from SG 1.350) and the pH adjusted accordingly.
o Initially mix 50:50 with hot water (add concentrate to water) and mix with air or re-circulating pump or with a 
mechanical mixer.

Juice Composition and the Components of Apple Juice
Apple juice is about 90% water and 10% soluble solids. There are a number of different scales used to measure the 
soluble solid concentration, however by convention Specific Gravity (SG) is the density scale used by cider makers. SG 
describes the relative density of a liquid compared to pure water, which is given a value of 1.000. As apple and pear 
juices contain substances in solution, they will have a higher S.G. (typically SG 1.045 – 1.065). It should be noted that 
alcohol has a lower S.G. than water. Alternatively, the term “Gravity” can be used, which is easily calculated from SG as  
follows:
•	 Gravity (Degrees0) = (S.G. x 1000) – 1000. Therefore, apple juice will typically have a gravity of 450 – 650
Degrees Brix and degrees Baume are other scales used to measure the soluble solid concentration of solutions. They are 
used by wine makers and for juice concentrates. T ables are used to convert between the different scales 
•	 e.g. S.G. 1.040 = 10.0o Brix = 5.60 Baume.
The main components of apple juice are as follows:
•	 Sugar makes up 80% of the soluble solids and, hence, is the main component that determines the SG of the juice. 
Sugars are subsequently converted by the yeast into alcohol during fermentation. 
•	 The main sugars in apple juice are:
Fructose - 60 gl-1
Sucrose - 30 gl-1

•	 Organic Acids. These compounds affect the pH and taste of the juice and cider. The main acid in apples is Malic

Quinic acid 0.4 – 5 gl-1
Citric < 0.2 gl-1 This acid can be found at higher concentrations in pear juice.

•	 Nitrogenous Compounds. The amount of soluble nitrogen can typically vary from almost zero to about 350 mg per 
litre and possibly higher in apples from heavily fertilized bush-orchards. The soluble nitrogen is mainly in the form 
of Amino-acids (primarily aspartic acid, glumatic acid and asparagine), with (unlike grapes) only trace amounts of 
inorganic nitrogen being present. These compounds are needed by the yeast as a primary nutrient. 
•	 Phenolics (T annins). The main ones are Chlorogenic acid, Phloridzin, Epicatechin, Procyanidin. All apples contain 
phenolics, but as detailed above, cider apples have much higher concentrations of these chemical compounds and  
therefore have a significant impact on the flavour of the apples and cider.
•	 Aroma Compounds. Although overall at very low concentrations compared with the other components of apple 
juice, these compounds provide the aroma and contribute to the flavour of apples. Between 80 – 90% of the aroma 
compounds found in apples are esters (e.g. ethyl acetate), with the particular mix determining the different aroma 
and flavour of each individual apple variety. Additionally – particularly in the aromatic apples – there are also a 
number of alcohols e.g. hexanol and trace amounts of aldehydes e.g. acetaldehyde. There is limited evidence to show 
that the original aroma compounds found in apples carry-through to the fermented cider and thus most of the aroma 
compounds found in cider are yeast-derived. 
•	 ‘B’ Group Vitamins. Although essential for yeast growth and activity (e.g. Vitamin B1 – thiamine and vitamin B5 – 
panthothenate), the concentration of these compounds vary considerably between varieties and seasons.  
•	 Mineral Salts. Along with nitrogenous compounds and vitamins, minerals are essential for yeast growth and activity. 
Variable amounts can be found in apple juice, correlated with the amount of the appropriate fertilizer added to the 
trees in the orchard. 
•	 Pectin. This is a jelly-forming substance that originates in the cell walls of apples and pears. It becomes soluble as 
the apple and pear ripens and is liberated when the fruit is milled. It thus can cause juice extraction problems when 
pressing the mash if found at a high concentration. 
Cider Microbiology
Cider making is a biotechnological process and, as such, the growth and activity of microorganisms are central to 
production. Simply put, the job of a cider maker is a microbiological controller.  
All readers are therefore strongly encouraged to gain as much knowledge and understanding about microbiology and 
biochemistry as possible (and continue to learn), in order to facilitate the production of high quality cider and perry on an 
ongoing basis and confidently deal with challenges as they arise and before they become problems. 
Fundamentals of Microbiology
A few basic principles first:
1. T wo main types of microorganisms can be found in cider and will, if present and active, have an impact on the 
product: Y easts, which are a type of fungi and bacteria, which are a simple group of prokaryotic organisms in 
their own right. Microorganisms are typically given Latin names e.g. Saccharomyces cerevisiae, the main yeast 
species that carries out the primary alcoholic fermentation and Lactobacillus collinoides, a rod-shaped species of 
bacteria that may cause a secondary “malo-lactic fermentation” but unfortunately can also produce off-flavours. 
2. Micro-organisms are very small: Yeasts measure about 5 – 40µm in diameter, where as bacteria are typically about 
1/10th of this and range from 0.5 – 5.0µm in size. Yeast cells are often ellipsoid in shape, but there are some that 
look different from this. Bacteria are normally either spherical (“cocci”) or rod-like (“bacilli”) but, again, different 
shapes can be found, though not commonly in cider.

3. Microbiology is a ‘numbers game’. The presence by itself of one single microbial cell will not have very much 
impact (positive or negative) on the environment (e.g. cider) in which it exists, but the more there are the greater 
effect they will have. For example, 100 million yeast cells per millilitre are required for fermentation to progress 
satisfactorily, whereas in many packaged ciders the requirement is to have none at all. It is therefore more 
common to talk about microbial populations - “T otal Viable Count” (TVC) or “microbial loading”.
4. Following on from above, microbial growth is mainly defined as an increase in viable cell numbers – one cell 
divides into two, two into four etc - and will happen in an exponential fashion if the conditions are conducive for 
the individual species concerned e.g. if the conditions are ideal, one yeast cell can grow into 10 million cells in 24 
hours!. The main factors that affect the ability of microorganisms to grow and survive are as follows:
a. Availability of water – if not present, microorganisms will typically not grow, but some can remain 
dormant. Usually, there is plenty of water available in the cider making process.
b. Some microorganisms require oxygen for growth and are termed as being “Aerobic” (e.g. Acetic Acid 
bacteria). For some microorganisms, oxygen is actually toxic (“Obligate Anaerobes”), but none of 
these are associated with cider production. Others are “Facultative Anaerobes” and are able to thrive 
whether oxygen is present or not, however they may ‘perform’ differently depending on the conditions 
e.g.  Saccharomyces cerevisiae requires oxygen for growth, but it will only start producing alcohol in 
anaerobic conditions. 
c. As living organisms, a range of nutrients are also required for growth and activity e.g. sugar as a source 
of energy and carbon skeletons, a nitrogen source for the production of proteins, along with vitamins 
& minerals. 
d. A suitable temperature. Most organisms will not grow and thrive if the temperature is too low and 
then will die if a high (“lethal”) temperature is reached. Within the normal ‘comfort zone’, the higher 
the temperature the quicker the organisms will grow and the more active they will become (e.g. a 
faster fermentation). In cider production, this so-called ‘comfort zone’ typically ranges from 100 – 
300 C, but is species dependant.
e. Microorganisms are significantly affected by the pH of the environment. Many yeasts and bacteria 
cannot grow and survive at the relatively low pH found in juice and cider. The lower the pH is the 
more challenging is the environment for the organisms concerned.
f. There are a number of chemical agents that have anti-microbial properties. The most important 
example of this is sulphur dioxide (SO2). Some of the microorganisms found in juice or cider are 
very susceptible to SO2 (e.g. Kloeckera apiculata) and others are not (e.g. Saccharomycodes ludwigii). 
Phenolics also have some anti-microbial properties, as does alcohol itself. Other anti-microbial agents 
are also permitted for use in cider, notably sorbates and dimethyldicarbonate (“Velcorin”).
5. When there is an existing population of viable microorganisms, when lethal temperatures are reached, the 
microorganisms do not all die at once; they die (just like they grow) in an exponential fashion. The higher 
the population density the longer it therefore takes for ‘microbial kill’ to occur. This is the background to 
heat pasteurisation. Theoretically, however, absolutely sterility can never be attained, but in practice sufficient 
microbial kill can be achieved to ensure that the treated product will be microbiologically stable.
6. Microorganisms can be found everywhere, but juice and cider should be viewed as a ‘selective growth media’ 
(mainly due to its relatively low pH) and thus only a limited number of different types will be found in juice and 
cider. The main yeasts and bacteria that can be found in juice and cider and can influence cider production are 
listed overleaf.

Microbial Control
Overall, the following main aspects require careful thought, attention and action:
• Good hygiene is good business
• Hygienic design & pest control
• Sanitisation
• pH, temperature and oxygen control
• Use of anti-microbial agents e.g. SO2
• Utilization of microbial competition
• Physical treatments – notably pasteurisation and (sterile) filtration
• Continual diligence

Sanitization
By definition, this is the WHOLE process involved in achieving a suitable standard of hygiene. It involves CLEANING 
and DISINFECTION.
Detergents & Disinfectants
It is important to understand the mode of action of all chemicals and to relate to the generic group. There are only a 
few main groups:
Detergents – used to CLEAN
1. Caustic T ypically based on the alkali sodium hydroxide (NaOH), caustic is widely used and does not have any 
major drawbacks – other than it reacts with CO2, which will reduce the effectiveness of the caustic and can cause a 
vacuum to be formed in a closed system during Cleaning-in-Place (CIP). Caustic can also strip paint and will split 
wood, thus it is not suitable for cleaning wooden barrels. 
2. Sodium Carbonate is mildly caustic and can be used to clean wooden barrels.
3. Acidic detergents are primarily based on phosphoric & nitric acids are typically used to brighten stainless. Also 
useful where there is a high concentration of residual CO2 inside a tank. 
Disinfectants – Used to reduce microbial populations to numbers below which they will not cause problems.
1. Chlorine. Use cold at between 150 – 250mgl-1 chlorine (typically supplied at 9 - 12%v/v - check strength before 
ordering because if it is cheap it may be weaker!). Relatively cheap and effective … BUT …it can badly affect the 
flavour of cider at very low concentrations and, to make matters worse, it does not rinse easily. 
2. Peroxyacetic Acid (PAA). This is the most widely used disinfectant in the Cider Industry and is also used cold. 
Although very pungent, it is “food safe” as it breaks down to water and a small amount of acetic acid. Although 
can be rinsed out with product, it will badly oxidize the cider, therefore it is best rinsed out with clean potable 
water before product is put into the tank or pipe or equipment that has been treated. It should also be noted that 
the stock solution supplied deteriorates over time. It is therefore best to use PAA test strips to help achieve the 
recommended final concentration (usually at 250mgl-1).  
The contact time is all-important with cold disinfectants. They may kill all known microorganisms, but how long does 
it take? T wenty minutes is a minimum, longer is much better.
Sanitization chemicals can be formulated into:
•	 Liquids
•	 Powders
•	 Foams
•	 Gels
For tanks and pipe-work, CIP is typically used. Pipe-work is re-circulated with the detergent and tanks are sprayed 
internally using a “Spray-ball” or “Fury”.
A typical sanitization routine may include the following sequence of activities:
1. Removal of gross dirt / debris
2. Water rinse
3. Clean (chemical and physical action) e.g. use a 6%v/v solution of a 30% cold caustic detergent (2.0% w/v

4. Water rinse
5. Disinfect (chemical) Typically dose PAA at 250mgl-1 with a contact time of 30 minutes at an ambient

6. Final rinse (potable water)

Sulfur Dioxide in Cider Production
History and Background
Prior to the 20th century sulfur used to be burnt in barrels to help keep them clean. The first documented recommendation 
for use in cider can be found in Evelyn’s Pomona in 1664, although it had been previously used by the Romans. Subsequently, 
detailed research into the use of sulfur dioxide (SO2) in cider making was carried out in the early 1950’s at Long Ashton. 
The main findings were:
•	 Most non-fermenting yeasts and spoilage bacteria removed; 
•	 Any added yeast cultures could then dominate fermentation and impose their own flavour characteristics
SO2 is still widely used in cider production and its controlled use is highly recommended.
Advantages of using SO2
a) Kills off many spoilage organisms, which can either result in specific infections and the production of unwanted 
taints.
b) Promotes a more efficient yeast fermentation either by ‘wild’ or selected yeasts 
c) It also has anti-oxidant properties and thus can help extend the shelf-life of packaged products
d) Helps prevent chill hazes.
e) Overall, it can result in a more standardized and predictable product being made.
Disadvantages of using SO2
a) Some asthmatics are very sensitive to SO2.  WHO recommend a maximum intake of 100mg per day for adults.  
This amount would be achieved by drinking a liter of cider containing 100mgl-1. However, it should be noted that 
SO2 is a widely used preservative in other foodstuffs.
b) Can inhibit a malo-lactic fermentation (MLF) by suppressing lactic acid bacteria, if MLF is required. 
c) Destroys Thiamine (vitamin B1).
d) A precursor for the possible formation of hydrogen sulfide (H2S – a “rotten-egg” smell) by yeast during 
fermentation.
All packaged products for retail sale require a sulfite declaration on the label if the product contains more than 10mgl-1 
T otal SO2. Even if SO2 is not added, yeasts can produce up to about 60 mgl-1 of the compound. 
Use of Sulfur Dioxide
When SO2 is added to juice or cider, then part of it will become bound up with other substances in the liquid with the rest 
remaining ‘Free’. Only that which is free is effective against micro-organisms. For example, juice from rotten fruit contains 
substances that will bind SO2 and thus will reduce its effectiveness. Furthermore, there is also a direct relationship between 
the pH of the juice or cider and the microbiologically ‘active’ molecular’ SO2 (a component of the free SO2). Essentially, 
the lower the pH, the less total SO2 is required to achieve its antimicrobial activity. 
It is commonly used at three main stages of the cider production process:
1. Prior to fermentation primarily to suppress wild yeasts & bacteria. 25-150 mgl-1 total SO2 is added dependant on 
pH & whether or not the juice has been pasteurised. 
2. On racking for the control of microflora during post-fermentation storage/maturation. More is added at this  
stage of the production process to target (typically) 30– 40 mgl-1 Free SO2.
3. On bottling. Its primary purpose at this stage is to minimize oxidation in the final pack and thus help extend 
shelf-life. It should be recognised that SO2, even in conjunction with Sorbates (another preservative that can be 
used) cannot be relied upon to suppress microbial growth and activity in pack. There are yeasts that are extremely 
resistant to SO2.
SO2 itself is a gas and is a significant health and safety risk if it is used like this, so it is usually added in the form of a

Fermentation
Overview
Although some, mainly small-scale, cider makers will carry out fermentation using ‘wild’ indigenous yeasts, the majority 
of producers will add selected strains  of yeast (Saccharomyces species) to help ensure that they can always achieve a 
consistent, clean and good flavoured product free from spoilage. Although some very good ciders can be produced 
through the activity of ‘wild’ yeasts, results are not predictable. Y east nutrients are also commonly added, a practice that 
also goes back for many hundred years when it was not uncommon in centuries past for a cider maker add a leg of mutton 
to “help fermentation”. The story about adding a dead rat, however, is a myth and these days, specially formulated yeast 
nutrients are available.
Fermentation will typically take 2 to 4 weeks (sometimes longer, sometimes shorter) during which time the yeast will 
convert the sugar found in apple juice to alcohol (ethanol) and carbon dioxide (CO2); the latter being vented out through 
an airlock in the vat. The yeast will also produce other flavour compounds  during fermentation – and their production 
will vary according to the strain of yeast used, one of the main causes of the different flavors found in different ciders. 
At the end of fermentation, unless an ‘arrested fermentation’ has been carried out to produce a ‘naturally sweet’ cider (not 
an easy practice and not commonly carried out), all the sugar will have been metabolised and the SG, when measured, will 
be at 1.000 or slightly below (alcohol has a lower SG than water). At this time the ‘raw’ cider will be carefully “racked” off 
(separated from) the “lees” (spent yeast cells and other debris found at the bottom of the fermentation vat) into a storage 
vessel for maturation.

Potential Alcohol Production
During fermentation, sugar is metabolized by the yeast to form roughly equal proportions of ethanol and CO2
The measurement of Specific Gravity (e.g. using a hydrometer) provides a reasonable guide to the amount of sugar 
present, hence the potential alcohol concentration (%ABV) can be estimated as follows:
Step 1: Estimate the likely drop in Gravity during fermentation. For the purposes of this rough calculation, always 
presume that the juice will stop fermenting at a Specific Gravity (SG) of 1.000 - e.g. if the initial SG is 1.050, 
then the likely drop in Gravity will be 50 degrees (500).
Step 2: % ABV = Gravity drop ÷ 7.5  e.g. 50  =  6.7% ABV
 7.5
Alternatively, a cider maker may well wish to target a specified alcohol concentration in the fermented cider and calculate 
the amount of extra sugar to be added to achieve this, a process called “Chapitalisation”. The necessary calculations are

Step 1: Calculate overall required Gravity change during fermentation:
 Overall required Gravity  = %ABV X 7.5
 Example: 10% ABV x 7.5 = 750
Step 2: Calculate the extra Gravity required over & above of what is already present in the juice:
 Extra Gravity = Required minus already present
 Example: 750 minus 500 = 250
Step 3:  Calculate the amount of sugar (in grams) required as follows:
 Amount sugar (g) = Fermentation volume (ltrs) x Gravity x 2.6.

pH Control 
Prior to fermentation, the juice should be analysed for its acid concentration and – SEPARATELY – its pH.  Although 
acid concentration and pH are interrelated, two separate data points should be determined. Each parameter provides 
different information: Simply, the measurement of acid concentration provides information about the actual amount of 
acid present (i.e. grams per litre concentration), whereas the pH provides an indication of the strength of the acid.
By definition an acid is a substance that liberates hydrogen ions when in solution: 
 e.g. Hydrochloric acid: HCl     H+ + Cl-
The concentration of an acid can be directly measured by titration (mixing) of an acid with an alkaline to produce a 
solution that is neutral (this point is determined), whereas pH is a measurement (using a pH meter or indicator strips) 
of how many hydrogen ions (H+) are liberated in a solution and thus is an indication of the strength of the acid present.  
Generally, but NOT always, the greater the acid concentration the more H+ ions are liberated.  pH is measured on a scale 
of 1 to 14; with pH 7.0 being neutral. The pH scale is inverse, so the more H + ions that are present, the lower is the 
reported pH value below pH 7.0.  
Often in apple juice and cider there is NOT a direct relationship between the amount of acid present and the amount of 
H+ ions released. This will depend on:
o An individual acid’s nature
o The variety of acids present in any given solution
o The amount and variety of other substances present
In high acid varieties of apple, most of the acid is malic acid and there is a close relationship between acid and the pH. But 
in low acid varieties, the mixture of acids is more complex and the relationship between total acid concentration and pH 
is less clearly defined i.e. juices with the same total acid concentration can show different pH’s and vice versa.
The ideal pH level in juice prior to fermentation should be between pH 3.3 - 3.5 (NO higher than pH 3.8). The best way 
to achieve this is by blending together different varieties of fruit, but this is not always possible. Alternatively, food grade 
malic acid can be added to decrease the pH or calcium carbonate (chalk) can be used to increase the pH. Unfortunately, 
it is practically impossible to calculate the amount of acid or chalk to add to the juice to predictably alter the pH by a 
specified amount. Therefore, you should aim to adjust the acid concentration towards an overall concentration (including 
the concentration of the existing acid in the original juice) of 5.0 - 6.0 gl-1 (measured as malic acid), then recheck the pH. 
Small scale trials should be used to determine the dose of malic acid or chalk to be used, before adding the overall amount 
to the total volume of juice that requires treatment.
Yeast Selection, Use and Management
The most widely used form of yeast culture used is the so-called “Active Dried Yeast” (ADY). A wide range of strains 
are available – most originally isolated from successful ‘wild yeast’ fermentations -  in a variety of pack sizes to suit all 
scales of production. ADY is usually added in conjunction with sulphiting and will often be necessary for fermentation to 
proceed successfully. However, producers can also add a culture without the use of SO2; aiming to out-compete any other 
microorganisms.
As a general guide, many producers find the so-called “Champagne” or white-wine yeasts strains the most successful ones 
for cider production, either used as a single strain or, occasionally, combining two or more strains together. T ypical dosage 
rates for ADY are 20 – 30g per hectoliter of juice. ADY will need careful re-hydration before it is added to the juice for 
fermentation, either (and preferably) using special yeast rehydration nutrients (follow manufacturer’s instructions), which 
are designed to supply the yeast with the necessary micronutrients (e.g. vitamins), or just by using water as follows:

1. Use potable tap water at 400C (no higher than 430C)
2. Gradually stir in the yeast – 10% w/v suspension e.g. 10g per 100ml.  Add yeast TO water, not vice versa
3. Stopper or cover container and leave for 20 minutes. Do not mix.

by running cold water over the outside of the contained used, or gradually adding the colder juice directly to 
the yeast suspension over about a 10 minute period. The maximum period of time from when the yeast is first 
added to the water and the suspension added to the juice should be no more than 30 minutes.
Yeasts require the following nutrients for growth & vitality and to complete a successful fermentation:
1. An energy source and carbon skeletons, which are obtained from sugar. Ethanol and CO2 are produced as 
metabolic by-products. 
If Chapitalisation is to be carried out, additional sugar ideally should be ‘batch-fed’ to the yeast during fermentation. As 
a guide, at every 100 gravity drop during fermentation, add up to 30gl -1 of sugar at any one time. This timing will often 
link to the timings for adding other yeast nutrients. Ensure the sugar is completely dissolved before adding to the “must”.
2. A source of nitrogen for the production of proteins, known as Y east Assimilable Nitrogen (YAN). Yeasts are able to 
utilize amino-acids (Free Amino Nitrogen – FAN) and also inorganic nitrogen (e.g. ammonium – NH4+). 
Apple juice contains amino-acids but, essentially, no inorganic nitrogen. The concentration of nitrogen found in juice is 
often not in sufficient quantities to meet the yeast’s requirements, therefore an additional source of nitrogen will normally 
be required, typically an extra 75 - 200mgl-1 N. The amount to add will depend on the initial level in the apple juice itself, 
the yeast strain used and the extent of fermentation.  Symptoms of nitrogen deficiency include the production of hydrogen 
sulfide (H2S – a ‘bad-egg smell’) and/or a stuck or sluggish fermentation.
Additional nitrogen will be supplied if using a proprietary yeast nutrient and also by using diammonium phosphate 
(DAP), which contains 21% w/w available nitrogen. T rials will be required to establish ideal nutrient regimes for different 
scenarios. 
3. Vitamins & minerals also play a crucial role in yeast growth & activity. The two main vitamins that can cause 
problems by their deficiency in cider fermentation are:
a) Thiamine (B1). This is also degraded by SO2. This vitamin can be added directly to the juice (< + 0.5mgl-1) or 
can be added as part of a proprietary yeast nutrient.
b) Pantothenate (B5). Deficiency is also associated with H2S formation. About 0.25mgl-1 is typically required; 
either added directly or as part of a proprietary yeast nutrient.
4. Oxygen is also crucial to ensure yeast growth in the early stages of fermentation and also to ensure the resulting 
yeasts cells are healthy. In practice, an effort should be taken to ensure that the fresh juice is thoroughly aerated 
prior to fermentation. 
The following points should also be noted:
•	 Seek manufacturer’s advice and instructions when using a proprietary blended yeast nutrient.
•	 As a guide, half of the proprietary yeast nutrient complex should be added to the juice about 6 - 12 hours after 
yeast addition and the other half about 1/3rd of the way through fermentation.
•	 DAP is toxic to yeasts whilst being re-hydrated and also, ideally, should not be added in the very early stage of 
fermentation. If DAP is being used, then add ‘little and often’, but aim to ensure that the whole dose is added in 
during the first half of fermentation.

A basic guide for managing fermentation is as follows:
1. Ensure the vessel is made from food-grade material and is effectively sanitized before use.
2. Use an air lock. Useful as a quick visual check to see that fermentation is progressing satisfactorily and will keep 
debris (and air, in the later stages of fermentation) out of the vessel, but allow CO2 to escape. 
3. Maintain an appropriate temperature in the fermentation vessel, matched to the individual yeast strain(s) being 
used. The normal temperature range for most fermentation yeasts is between 120C - 230C.  
4. Regularly (at least daily) monitor fermentation progress: S.G.and temperature. It is also important keep a check on 
any production of H2S as this is often indicative of nutrient deficiency and other “stress” factors.
5. Undertake yeast nutrition, as outlined above.
A number of factors can affect the rate of fermentation and the following list can also be used as a trouble-shooting guide 
in case problems emerge (e.g. slow or stuck fermentations):
1) Y east numbers and strain. If an ADY has not been added or incorrectly handled, it may well be that there are 
not sufficient numbers of viable yeast cells present.
2) T emperature. In practice this is often the main reason why fermentation can be sluggish or “stuck”. Ideally, 
if the juice is warm enough (150 – 200 C dependant on yeast strain) before the yeast is added, then as 
fermentation is exothermic, often sufficient heat will be generated to keep the yeast active. It should be noted, 
however, in large fermentation tanks excess heat can build-up, therefore chilling may well be needed.
3) Y east nutrient deficiency. A lack of nutrients can lead to a sluggish or stuck fermentation, even if no H2S is 
generated at the same time. 
4) Oxygen. Although it is essential to ensure anaerobic conditions during most of fermentation, yeasts do require 
oxygen to grow up to form a healthy population during the early stages of fermentation.

Fermentation End & Racking
Fermentation typically ends when all the sugars have been metabolized (“dryness”). In cider, the S.G. will fall to 1.000 or, 
in the case of relatively high alcohol ciders, below 1.000. There will be no further changes to S.G. It should be noted, that 
in perry, due to the likelihood of it containing un-fermentable sugar (sorbitol), fermentation may finish at a S.G. above 
1.000
Yeast and other solid material will flocculate and settle to the bottom of the vessel to form the lees, leaving the raw cider 
relatively clear. One criterion that should be used when selecting a yeast is to choose one that will flocculate well. Poorly 
flocculating strains will leave a cloudy cider, which may be difficult to stabilize. Low temperature will stimulate the 
flocculation process.
After the end of fermentation it is important to rack the clear raw cider off the lees. Usually this should be done as soon 
as possible so as to minimize the effects of yeast autolysis (notably the formation of unpleasant smelling organic sulphur 
compounds from these ‘rotting’ yeast cells). The aim should be to rack the cider within a maximum of 2 weeks after the 
end of fermentation and much earlier if possible. Racking at the end of fermentation will also produce a more stable 
product; less likely to become infected by unwanted micro-organisms.
Key objectives at racking are to:
• Minimize waste and maximize cider recovery.
• Avoid aeration as far as possible and retain dissolved CO2. Oxygen pick-up at this stage can lead to excessive 
oxidation of the product and may also lead to microbial spoilage of the cider (e.g. acetification) in storage.
• Ensure safety of personnel – remember that CO2 gas can lead to asphyxiation.
• Do not collapse the vessel when emptying it! Remember, when you draw-off the cider, the volume in the tank 
that it took up will need to be replaced by something else; so open up a tank vent.
• Assure good standards of hygiene – Ensure the reception vessel and transfer pipes have been effectively 
sanitised.
• Carry the process out efficiently & effectively
At the time of racking, the cider will often be sampled and analysed for SG, total & free SO2, total acidity and pH. 
The pH will be re-adjusted if necessary and usually the cider is also re-sulfited to give ca. 30mg l-1 free SO2. The 
cider may also be:
 
•	  Fined
•	 Centrifuged
•	 Coarse filtered
•	 Blended
•	 Diluted, sweetened and re-fermented if the cider has a significant unbalanced flavour
After about 4 days, a representative sample of the recently racked cider should undergo a full analytical assessment for: 
Alcohol content, total acidity, pH, SG and free & total SO2. A sensory analysis should also be undertaken.

Maturation
Overview
In overview, the raw fermented cider will significantly benefit from a period of maturation during which time the 
following will or may occur:
•	 Overall, the cider will improve in flavour – it will become less harsh and more ‘rounded’, smooth and complex 
in flavour. A key job of the cider maker during the period of maturation - which can last for a few weeks through 
to several months depending on the type of apple used and the style of cider being made - is to regularly taste the 
cider until it is judged ready for final processing. A careful check will also be taken to look out for any signs of 
spoilage, with a range of steps then being taken to prevent problems worsening.
•	 Malo-lactic fermentation (MLF) may take place. This process is undertaken by lactic acid bacteria, which convert 
the malic acid derived from apples to lactic acid and CO2. Lactic acid is only half the strength of malic acid, so the 
resulting cider will taste less sharp. In addition, the bacteria concerned can also produce a range of other flavoured 
chemicals that can add to the overall complexity of the product. MLF can occur spontaneously and some producers 
will actively encourage it through the use of selected strains of bacteria. Depending on the style of cider to be 
produced and the methods employed by the individual maker concerned, others will choose to prevent MLF. Some 
further information is included below.
•	 With cider that is made from cider apples that contain high levels of tannins, a number of natural chemical changes 
will occur (along with concomitant changes in color), which will typically result in the cider tasting less bitter and 
harsh. Additional particulate matter, found in suspension in the freshly fermented and racked raw cider, will also 
sediment to the bottom of the storage tank and will thus result in the maturing cider becoming fairly clear.
During the period of maturation, individual ciders may be blended and, in certain situations, a fining agent such as 
bentonite or gelatine may be added to improve the clarity or flavour of the cider concerned. Once judged ready by the 
cider maker, the matured “base” cider will usually go through a range of final downstream processing steps prior to 
packaging. 
Maturation Management
Key considerations are as follows:
1. As introduced above, bulk cider & perry do benefit from a period of post-fermentation storage (maturation). 
Although there is no recommended defined length of time for this, then the following should act as initial 
guidelines:
a. For cider made from cider apples, maturation benefit can typically accrue over a 4 to 6 month period 
to allow time for the apple phenolics to polymerize and thus change in sensory characteristic.
b. For cider made from dessert apples and perry from dessert-type pears (not perry pears), one month 
may be enough time, but this will depend the style of cider required e.g. if the cider maker wants 
wine-like characteristics to develop, extended maturation periods will be required.
c. For perry made from perry pears, then many producers find extended maturation periods are required 
– possibly up to between 12 – 18 months.
2. During maturation, steps should be taken to ensure NO air contact. The only safe way to achieve this is to keep 
storage vessels completely FULL. Bag-in-box storage systems are very useful for this purpose, but do ensure 
that the liner is compatible with cider. Alternatively, blanketing the storage tank head-space with inert gas 
can help e.g. CO2 (heavier than air, but does dissolve in the cider), nitrogen (is not heavier than air) or argon 
(expensive, but heavier than air and does not dissolve in the cider). Notwithstanding the above comments, 
there is some evidence to suggest that, particularly cider made from cider apples, may benefit from controlled 
micro-oxygenation during storage.
3. Unless you are aiming to encourage a malo-lactic fermentation, then the cider should be stored at a 
temperature between about 100 to 150 C.
4. Aim to maintain a free SO2 concentration of 30mgl-1 in the cider, unless you are aiming to encourage MLF.
5. Most importantly, the cider should be regularly sampled and monitored during the period of maturation to 
determine when it is acceptable for downstream processing and packaging for the marketplace and to keep a 
close check on any off-flavours starting to develop, along with any unwanted changes. In addition to assessing 
the sensory properties of the cider concerned, analysis for total acidity, volatile acidity, free & total SO2 and 
possibly diacetyl may be undertaken from time to time. Also look out for any microbial activity (e.g. gas 
release) that may indicate the cider is possibly suffering from unwanted yeast or bacterial activity.

Malo-Lactic Fermentation
Malo-lactic fermentation (MLF) is carried out by lactic acid bacteria (LAB) if the conditions are conducive for these 
bacteria to grow and thrive. LAB are typically always present, so the likelihood of MLF occurring is high unless active 
steps are taken to inhibit their activity. The main chemical change that occurs is the conversion of malic acid to lactic acid 
plus CO2. A range of other flavour compounds will also be produced.
Although MLF can bring about some positive changes to cider, many strains of the bacteria concerned are also responsible 
for a number of the potential sensory faults found in cider e.g. excess diacetyl (a sickly ‘butterscotch’ flavour), acetification 
in perry, “ropiness / oiliness”, excess volatile phenolics (“Barnyard”, medicinal and sweaty leather horse saddle flavours), 
ethyl catechol (musty), excess biogenic amines and “mousiness”. Very good cider can be made without MLF and, unless 
the process is very carefully monitored and controlled, including the use of selected strains of LAB, then the risk of 
spoilage by allowing a spontaneous MLF is too high.
The following factors will influence the potential occurrence of MLF:
•	 Usually occurs towards the end of fermentation, or in store
•	 More likely to occur when cider left in contact with lees
•	 Commonly associated with storage of cider in wooden barrels
•	 Usually only occurs when temperature reaches 150C and above
•	 Maintenance of a pH between 3.3 – 3.5 will help suppress the activity of the main LAB strains that can 
produce unwanted off-flavours
•	 SO2 strongly inhibits bacteria – Effective use of SO2,  in conjunction with pH and temperature control, 
during storage of the cider (i.e. maintain a concentration of at least 30mgl-1 Free SO2) is a key to 
preventing spontaneous MLF
•	 High alcohol can inhibit change
•	 Some yeast strains inhibit MLF
Non-Microbial Changes During Maturation
A number of chemical changes can also occur to the chemical compounds found in cider during the period of maturation 
as follows: 
1. Phenolics (T annins)
•	 Polymerization (primarily of the procyanidins) occurs resulting in a reduction in bitterness. Initially, astringency 
can increase due to the production of medium-sized polymers but will then decrease.
•	 Generally, the concentration will decrease as phenolics bind and precipitate with proteins and cell debris.
•	 When cider is stored in oak, wood phenolics can be extracted. These can add to the astringent and bitter flavour 
of cider, although it should be noted that wood tannins do not seem to ‘marry well’ with cider or perry. However, 
as oak barrels / vats are reused, extraction decreases. With the decreasing use of oak maturation vats, the use of 
oak chips may be considered. Although the oxidation effect of wooden vat ageing is lost, wood phenolics are 
extracted and you will be able to claim that the cider has undergone “oak maturation”! 
2. Acid
•	 In addition to the effect of the MLF on acid levels in cider, organic acids found in cider can also be chemically 
changed (esterified - as below) leading to decreased sourness.
3. Esters
•	 Fruity esters, produced as a result of the yeast metabolism of acids and alcohols, tend to be hydrolyzed back to 
the original substrates. Hence, fruitiness will decrease with time. However, chemical esterification of acids with 
alcohols (e.g. ethyl succinate) will often occur over time, resulting in a more wine-like character being formed in  
the stored cider.

Perry Production
Essentially the production of perry is the same as for cider; however there are certain differences and the following points 
need to be borne in mind:
•	 If possible, only use ‘proper’ perry pears. If using dessert pears, better results may be obtained by using pear 
concentrate (PJC) instead of fresh pear juice, as the evaporation process involved in making PJC removes some of 
the potential off-flavoured volatile components found in some pears.
•	 Some varieties of pears require immediate processing after harvest, others will benefit from a significant period of 
storage. It is often very difficult to judge the ideal time for processing – you may only have a 24-hour ‘window’ 
during which time the pears are just right for processing. Pears rot from the inside, therefore rotting can be 
difficult to spot.
•	 Whereas apples float, pears sink in water, therefore your water handling system may need modification
•	 Pears can contain considerable quantities of un-fermentable sorbitol, which will often result in “dry” fermented 
perry having a residual high S.G. and tasting quite sweet. With respect to initial estimates for alcohol concentration 
after fermentation and calculations for sugar additions for possible chapitalisation if required, estimate that the 
fully attenuated perry will be at a final SG of 1.005. Sorbitol is also a laxative and some people are quite sensitive 
to its effects. In some countries of the world, there is a requirement to label-declare the presence of sorbitol.
•	 Pears contain considerable quantities of citric acid which can be converted anaerobically to acetic acid by Lactic 
Acid Bacteria. Thus it is usually recommended to completely avoid MLF in perry.
•	 Pears also contain considerable quantities of acetaldehyde, which will bind SO2. Therefore you need to add 
additional SO2 to the juice for it to be effective - add an extra 50mgl-1 over and above the amount you would 
normally add to apple juice prior to fermentation and to cider.
•	 Perry from perry pears can be difficult to clear and can be very astringent due to the presence of complex phenolic 
compounds. Perry will often respond well to gelatin fining.
•	 Perry pears from long-established traditional orchards often contain very low levels of nitrogen therefore will often 
require routine supplementation.
•	 Due to the typical subtle, but complex flavours typically found in perry, these sensory properties can suffer badly 
even from slight oxidation or the presence of any other organoleptic faults.
