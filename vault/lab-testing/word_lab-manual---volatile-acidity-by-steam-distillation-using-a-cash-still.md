## Volatile Acidity by Steam Distillation Using a Cash Still


Volatile acidity (VA) is a catch-all term for those acids that may be distilled from cider or juice. In practice we are most concerned with the spoilage compound acetic acid (vinegar) and its ethanol ester, ethyl acetate. Aside from the potential detrimental sensory effects of volatile acidity, there are often legal limits to how much is allowed..

Steam distillation is a common method used to determine the level of volatile acidity in cider and juice. The acids are volatized from the cider by heating the sample in a still. They are then collected in a distillate. The acid content of the distillate can then be determined by titration with a standardized base. The results are reported in terms of g/L acetic acid.


### Interferents


Both CO2 and SO2 can be transferred to the distillate and, when dissolved, form titratable acids (carbonic and sulfuric respectively). To remove CO2 interference, the samples are degassed before testing. To remove interference from SO2, the sample is treated with an oxidant (H2O2 – hydrogen peroxide) to fix the SO2 into non-volatile sulphuric acid.

#### Blank Run


Every Cash still has its own unique amount of volatile acids that are present in the system due to improperly cleaned sample chambers, non-degassed water, or even bacterial activity in dead spaces. This means a sample of pure distilled water will still give a signal. This ‘blank’ value must be determined at the beginning of a day of VA determinations. Run this procedure at least two times. If your blanks agree, then proceed with testing your sample. If your blank values do not agree, run it a third time. Most often, the first run will be highest and will ‘blow off’ any non-background volatile contaminants accumulated since the last time the still was used. Once two determinations agree, consider that your blank value and move on to testing your sample. Don’t forget to subtract this blank value from the final value you determine for your samples.

Turn on condenser cooling water.

Make certain the clamps on the water drainage hose are securely closed.

Fill boiling chamber approximately 2/3 full with degassed dH2O ensuring the heating coil is completely submerged.

Volumetrically transfer 10 mL of distilled, degassed water to the sample chamber.

Add approximately 1 mL of 0.3% hydrogen peroxide.

Rinse the sample funnel with a bit of distilled, degassed water.

Ensure the stopcock is returned to the closed position.

Place the flexible rubber tubing from the condenser into the bottom of a 100 mL volumetric flask surrounded by an ice bath.

Place several drops of neutralized water in the bottom of the flask.

Turn on the power supply.

Collect about 100 mL of distillate.

Remove the volumetric flask containing the distillate.

Place a beaker below the condenser tube to collect any additional distillate.

Turn off the power supply.

Open the stopcock to equalize the pressure.

Add 2-3 drops of phenolphthalein to the distillate and titrate with standardized 0.1 N NaOH to a pale pink endpoint (Figure 11).

Open the sample drainage stopcock and allow sample to be sucked out of the inner chamber.

Close the sample drainage stopcock.

Add approximately 15 mL of degassed, dH2O to rinse the inner chamber.

Open the sample drainage stopcock to remove rinse water from the inner chamber.

Repeat rinsing 2 more times.

Close the sample drainage stopcock.
