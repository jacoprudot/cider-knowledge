## Reducing Sugars – Lane Eynon Method


Wet chemistry methods for the determination of the amount of sugar in a cider rely upon the ability of some sugars to reduce another chemical. For example, in the Lane-Eynon method, sugars reduce copper (II) to copper (I) oxide. Only some sugars, however, are capable of these reducing reactions, and are called reducing sugars. Reducing sugars include most monosaccharides, such as fructose and glucose. It is very important to note, however, that the disaccharide sucrose (table sugar) is not a reducing sugar. As well, there are many unfermentable sugars in cider, such as sorbitol, which are also not reducing sugars. The Lane-Eynon method is incapable of measuring the concentration of these non-reducing sugars. This is the difference between reducing sugar content and residual sugar content.

If the amount of sucrose in a cider sample needs to be determined, samples can be inverted (sucrose converted to fructose and glucose) and separately titrated to determine the sucrose concentration in the sample. Samples are inverted by the addition of the enzyme invertase or by boiling with strong acid.

This method reacts sugars with excess alkaline cupric sulphate. In the procedure, the cupric sulphate is titrated by a standardized dextrose solution to an endpoint that is signaled by the appearance of red copper oxide precipitate and the disappearance of blue from the methylene blue indicator. This is considered a reaction blank. The reaction is then repeated with the addition of the cider sample to the cupric sulphate. The difference between the volume required to titrate the cupric sulphate alone and required to titrate the cupric sulphate with cider is directly related to the reducing sugar content of the cider sample.


### Equipment


500 mL wide-mouth Erlenmeyer flasks

Hot plate/stirrer

Stir bars

10 mL volumetric pipettes and pipette pump

Retort stand and burette clamps

50 mL burette

100 mL graduated cylinder

p1000 micropipette and tips


### Reagents


Fehling’s A solution

Fehling’s B solution

0.5% (w/v) dextrose solution

1% (w/v) methylene blue indicator


#### Blank Titration


Only one blank titration value is need for all samples; complete blank titration in duplicate or until two readings differ by less than 0.2 mL.

Add 70 mL of dH2O to a 500 mL wide-mouth flask.

Place a stir bar into the flask.

Add 10 mL of Fehling’s A solution to the flask.

Add 10 mL of Fehling’s B solution to the flask and mix well.

Add dextrose solution into the 50 mL burette.

Put the hotplate/stirrer underneath the burette and preheat.

Place the flask on the preheated hot/plate stirrer and start stirring (low speed).

Record the initial volume of dextrose solution in the burette.

Titrate with approximately 18 mL of titrant.

When the Fehling’s solutions come to a rapid boil, add 5 drops of methylene blue indicator.

Continue to titrate dropwise until the end point is reached.

The end point is detected as the first disappearance of blue indicator and appearance of red precipitate (Figure 17).

This titration should not take more than 3 minutes to complete.

Record the volume of dextrose used (blank titration should require about 21.8 mL of dextrose).


#### Sample Titration


Add 70 mL of dH2O to a 500 mL wide-mouth flask.

Place a stir bar into the flask.

Add 1.0 mL of sample to the flask.

Continue from #4 in the blank titration method.

# Part 3: Microbiological Testing


There are many reasons to use microbiological testing on juice and cider. Most often, however, producers will want to determine the viable cell count (number of living cells) in a finished package to validate their microbial control methods.

What follows is a collection of techniques that may be helpful in general microbial evaluation. After that, the method for total viable cell counts is presented.

Doing in-house microbial testing of juice and cider requires different equipment, techniques, and knowledge as that of chemical testing. Paramount amongst these is the concept of aseptic technique. Aseptic technique ensures that no microbes from the environment contribute to false positives.
