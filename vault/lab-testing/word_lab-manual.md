# Part 1: Introduction


I’ve heard two reasons from cidermakers why they don’t do regular chemical or microbiological testing of their ciders. The first reason is practical: “I don’t know how”, or, “I don’t have the right equipment or reagents” (look for definitions of words that are bolded in the Glossary). This manual directly addresses these concerns and will help build confidence in testing cider in the lab – whatever that ‘lab’ space might be. The second reason cidermakers don’t do more analyses is a bit more philosophical: “I don’t want to make cider by the numbers” or “I make cider using only my palate”. For some, cider is an expression of artisanal effort; a creative endeavor informed by history, the harvest, and, most importantly, the cidermaker’s creative and aesthetic choices.

Putting aside the legal requirements for analyzing ciders, regular analysis can be key for producing unfaulted, consistent product for all types of cidermakers. Chemical and microbiological testing play an important role in Quality Assurance (QA), Quality Control (QC), product development, and food safety in cider production. Some cidermakers, however, feel that ‘doing the chemistry’ stifles the creative process. But this is a false dichotomy. Craft producers, large market producers, those that are both, and those that are neither all benefit from some level of analytical testing. Results from the lab give cidermakers the signposts they need to navigate the path towards the best product their raw ingredients, imagination, and palates are capable of.

Think back to when you were young and trying to draw a picture of, say, a car. How disappointing was it when your best efforts to draw the shiny Ferrari in your imagination produced the proverbial grade school drawing. It may have been recognizable as a car, but it didn’t match the image you had in your head – you were unable to bring your vision to life. It is a very frustrating experience when our creative output doesn’t meet our expectations.

High quality analytical testing, coupled with well-trained sensory evaluation, are the tools that producers use to make the cider they want to make, not just the cider they happen to make. These tools turn, “I’m pretty happy with how this turned out” into, “This is exactly what I wanted to create”. The purpose of this manual is to make sure cidermakers get the right analytical results, from the right test, at the right time to fulfill their creative and quality goals.

Great: testing juice or cider is a good thing. But before running out and building an in-house lab or sending out samples to a commercial lab for analysis, there are a few questions to consider first. Usually, when cider is tested there will be some sort of result (most often a number). So what? What does that number mean, from a cidermaking perspective? Before testing anything, every cidermaker should ask themselves two questions:

What action will I take as based upon this result?

How much will I trust this result?

The first question can only be answered by the cidermaker. Testing a parameter of the juice or cider “just to see” is a waste of time and money. For every test, there should be an action plan. Usually there will be an acceptable range for the result, based upon quality and sensory goals. When the result falls outside of this range, the cidermaker acts. Other times the cidermaker may need a full chemical or microbiological profile of a juice that they’ve not worked with before to help them envision what the final cider will taste like. The bottom line? If you don’t know why you are testing something, you’ll never know what to do with the result.

While it is up to the cidermaker to answer the first question, this manual is dedicated to answering the second question. There are several factors that impact the reliability of results. Some factors are easily controlled by best practices, while others might be more nuanced. Every cidermaker will have decided how much uncertainty they can live with based upon how critical and specific the action plan is for a particular analysis.
