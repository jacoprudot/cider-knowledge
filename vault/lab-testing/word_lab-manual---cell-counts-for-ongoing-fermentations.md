# word_lab manual - Cell counts for ongoing fermentations

> Source: Cider Institute of North America course materials

## Cell counts for ongoing fermentations


If you need to know the number of cells in your rehydrating yeast culture or in your inoculated fermentation, you can measure that directly by putting your sample into a haemocytometer and counting. You have the option of completing a total cell count (all the cells that are present), or a total viable cell count (all the cells that are able to remove methylene blue dye from inside the cell – ostensibly the cells that are alive). The haemocytometer is used when a sample is known to contain a relatively high concentration of cells. It is not useful when determining the cell population of samples with expected low concentrations, such as in finished, packaged product. In that case use the method for Total Viable Count for Finished, Packaged Products.


### Equipment


Microscope

Haemocytometer

Micropipette

Microcentrifuge tubes


### Reagents


0.1% methylene blue stain

0.2 M pH 7 Phosphate buffer


### Total Cell Count


Mix your sample and load 20 µL into the haemocytometer. The counting chamber will only hold 10 µL; ensure the sample fills the entire chamber.

If the cell concentration is too high to count, dilute the sample until it is possible to perform a count.

Count all the cells within the haemocytometer counting grid.

Calculate the cell concentration of the original culture (correct for any dilutions).


### Viable Cell Count


In a fresh Eppendorf tube mix:

400 µL phosphate buffer

100 µL sample

500 µL methylene blue stain

***Don’t forget that this is a 1/10 dilution***

Mix the contents of the Eppendorf tube and load onto the haemocytometer.

If the cell concentration too high to count, dilute the sample.

Do not count the cells that appear dark blue/black through-out; these are non-viable cells. Cells with blue staining at the edges should be counted (see Figure 2).

If cells are budding and both cells are roughly the same size, count as 2 cells. If one cell is noticeably smaller than the other, count as 1 cell.

Calculate the viable cell concentration of the original culture (correcting for all dilutions).


### Troubleshooting


Completing the viable cell count requires some experience and interpretation. To prevent false negatives (cells appear non-viable when they are, in fact, viable), cells should be tested only while they are in the exponential growth phase. As well, if the cells are left in contact with the methylene blue stain for an extended time, viable cells will start to appear non-viable. See Figure 21 for some guidance on interpretation.
