## Error


Accuracy and precision are both affected by error. There are three types of errors that plague laboratory analysis: gross errors, systemic errors, and random errors.

Gross errors are what first come to mind when thinking about the term ‘error’. These are large mistakes caused by carelessness or equipment failure. Gross errors are the best kind of error because they are usually very apparent. They can be noticed when the error is made, such as when too much reagent is added or there are results that make no sense for what is known about the sample. Gross errors are the easiest errors to correct as well, usually with renewed attention to detail, training, new equipment, or fresh reagents.

Systemic errors are errors that affect accuracy. These are built into the method and equipment. These errors usually cannot be removed, they are just facts of life. However, validation of the method against known samples can indicate the size and direction of these errors (i.e. the error always gives a high or low result – called positive or negative bias). Systemic errors are consistent and don’t change over time or with a change in technician or reagents. Systemic error is ‘baked-in’ to the method. It is up to the cidermaker to decide if the systematic error is acceptable or if a new method or equipment is needed.

Interferents are substances or conditions which can cause systemic errors and prevent accurate results. This can be tricky, because most interferents don’t prevent the method from giving a reasonable result, but they can compromise the accuracy of that result. If not corrected for, these interferents can shift your results higher or lower than they should be. Through-out this manual, any interferents in these methods will be explicitly stated along with ways to prevent that interference.

Random errors are the errors associated with precision. These are most often, but not always, due to human variation in measurement. Increasing the precision (degree of increment or graduation) can reduce the amount of random error, as the amount of estimation required by the technician becomes smaller and smaller. Training and technician conscientiousness also helps reduce random error, as can ensuring the freshness of reagents, cleanliness of glassware, and maintenance of equipment.

Random error can also result from changing conditions in the environment of a test. Consider a test that is sensitive to changes in pressure. On a stormy day, when the air pressure is fluctuating, this will introduce small, uncontrollable changes to the results of the test. We might not be able to control the weather, but other conditions of testing that might affect the results, such as temperature fluctuations, vibrations, or lighting can be controlled to help reduce random error.

One final note about accuracy and precision. If there is a +/- 5% uncertainty in the method (accuracy) and +/- 5% uncertainty in the measurements (precision). These two uncertainties are not additive. That is, the overall deviation from the ‘true’ value is not +/- 10%. In the case of 5% validation uncertainty and 5% measurement uncertainty, the cumulative uncertainty would actually be somewhere around 7%.

Accuracy and precision are the information points that address the second question that should be asked of every lab test – including results that come from commercial labs –  “How much will I trust this result?”. Accuracy helps to understand how much to trust the method and precision helps to understand how much to trust the measurement.
