## Free and Total Sulfur Dioxide


When sulfur dioxide (SO2) is added to juice or cider a portion becomes bound to phenolics, pigments, and other carbon containing compounds. Measuring SO2 levels is important as there is no way to predict how much added SO2 will be present in the free or bound forms. Only a very small portion of the Free SO2 in cider is in the form of Molecular SO2 , which is the form that is responsible for microbial protection.


### The Ripper Method


The Ripper method for SO2 determination is based on a reduction-oxidation (redox) reaction between Free SO2  and Iodine. The endpoint of the reaction is visualized when the Iodine has consumed all the Free SO2 in the sample and begins to react instead with a starch indicator. The Iodine-starch complex is dark blue/black in color. Once the blue/black color is stable, the endpoint has been reached.

Free SO2 present in the sample is determined directly using the Ripper method. To determine the Total SO2 present in the sample, the Bound SO2 must first be converted to Free SO2 by adding concentrated sodium hydroxide to the sample to reach a pH of 12. The sample is then re-acidified to carry out the titration with Iodine.


#### Interferents


The titration is done under acidic conditions to avoid consumption of the Iodine in oxidation reactions and helps to drive the reaction forward. Sodium bicarbonate is added to the reaction prior to titration to form a blanket of carbon dioxide in the flask to prevent interference of oxygen in the Iodine titration.

#### Determination of Free Sulfur Dioxide


Volumetrically transfer 25 mL of sample to a 250 mL flask.

Add approx. 5 mL of starch indicator and a pinch of bicarbonate.

Add approx. 5 mL of 25% (v/v) sulfuric acid.

Record the starting volume of the standard Iodine solution in the burette.

Rapidly titrate with standard Iodine solution to a blue endpoint (Figure 12).

Record the end volume of the standard Iodine solution in the burette.


#### Determination of Total Sulfur Dioxide Content


Volumetrically transfer 25 mL of sample to a 250 mL flask.

Add approx. 5 mL of 1.0 N NaOH, swirl, stopper and wait 5 minutes.

Add 5 mL starch indicator and a pinch of bicarbonate.

Add approx. 5 mL of 25% (v/v) sulfuric acid.

Titrate with Iodine solution as per free sulfur dioxide determination.

### Aeration/Oxidation (A/O)


The aeration/oxidation method is also frequently called the aspiration method. Passing a stream of air through an acidified juice or cider sample removes the Free SO2. The SO2 passes into a hydrogen peroxide trap where an oxidation reaction occurs.

The volume of standardized sodium hydroxide required to titrate the resulting sulfuric acid is used to calculate the Free SO2 levels. The same sample can then be heated which, in combination with the presence of a strong acid, causes Bound SO2 to dissociate. This sample can then be further aspirated. The released SO2 experiences the same fate as the Free SO2 in the first assay, being trapped and oxidized in the hydrogen peroxide trap. The amount of Bound SO2 can then be determined by titration with sodium hydroxide.


#### Interferents


Flow rate is extremely important in the aeration/oxidation method. If the rate is too low, not all of the Free SO2 will be carried into the hydrogen peroxide trap. If the flow rate is too high, SO2 may escape the hydrogen peroxide trap. The ideal flow rate is about 1 litre/minute.

Acetic acid and other volatile acids may be carried in the air stream and deposited in the flask containing hydrogen peroxide. This would artificially acidify the hydrogen peroxide. The air stream is passed through a cooling condenser to ensure that any volatile acids are condensed out of the air stream and fall back into the sample.

#### Determination of Free Sulfur Dioxide Content


Fill a burette with standardized 0.01 N NaOH solution (see method for standardizing NaOH in the Titratable Acidity method – remember that you are standardizing 0.01 N solution here – adjust the method accordingly).

Remove the pear-shaped hydrogen peroxide glass trap.

Add 10 mL of 0.3% (w/v) H2O2 and 1 to 2 drops of the mixed indicator solution into the hydrogen peroxide glass trap.

From the burette add a few drops of 0.01 N NaOH until the solution turns an olive green color which persists (Figure 13); it is not necessary to record this addition.

Reconnect the hydrogen peroxide glass trap.

Disconnect the round bottomed sample flask from the apparatus and add approximately 10 mL of o-phosphoric acid to the flask.

Volumetrically pipette 20 mL of sample into the sample flask.

Reconnect the sample flask.

Turn on the water flow through the condenser.

Turn on the aspiration and adjust the air flow to an appropriate rate.

Aspirate for 12 minutes.

Turn off the air pump and remove the hydrogen peroxide glass trap.

Using a squirt bottle rinse the delivery tube into the hydrogen peroxide glass trap with deionized water.

Record the initial burette reading.

Titrate the solution to an olive green color endpoint (Figure 14).

Record the final burette reading.

Figure 14: Aeration/oxidation titration colors. Number 1: Over-titrated; Number 2: When SO2 is trapped in the Hydrogen Peroxide Trap; Number 3: Endpoint


#### Determination of Bound Sulfur Dioxide Content


Replace the hydrogen peroxide glass trap.

Preserve the sample that was used to determine free SO2 in the sample flask.

Ensure there is water flow in the condenser.

Turn on the aspiration and adjust the air flow to an appropriate rate.

Light a Bunsen burner positioned under the round bottomed flask and adjust to maintain a gentle boil.

Aspirate for 12 minutes.

Titrate as with Free SO2 determination.
