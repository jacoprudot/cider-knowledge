## Basic Techniques


There are several techniques that are common to many analyses and it is important to become familiar with these techniques. For most methods there is unavoidable systemic error, however ensuring proper technique through familiarity with these basic techniques will avoid introducing large amounts of random error to results as well.


### Pipetting


Pipetting is a volumetric activity. That is, pipetting transfers an exact volume of solution or sample. The exception is when using a ‘transfer pipette’ which is an eye-dropper like device for delivering small amounts of solutions in approximate volumes.

Ensure the pipette is clean. If droplets of water or solution noticeably adhere to the inside of the pipette or if physical debris is visible, clean the pipette before use. If there is residual water from cleaning or other liquid from previous uses of the pipette, fill the pipette and discard with the liquid that will be measured three times.

Ensure the liquid being transferred is at the calibration temperature of the pipette (usually 20 °C). The pipette is fine-tuned to deliver the exact right volume at the calibration temperature. Even slight variations from the calibration temperature can create error in the result.

Fill the pipette. There are bulbs, pumps, and electronic fillers that can be used to draw liquid into the pipette. Make sure the tip of the pipette is fully immersed in the liquid and not touching the sides or bottoms of the glass.

Draw liquid into the pipette all the way up to the fill line. The bottom of the meniscus should rest upon the fill line (see Figure 4).

Remove the pipette from the liquid. Touch the tip of the pipette to the inside wall of the vessel holding your liquid. This ensures no drops of liquid on the outside of the pipette tip are transferred to the receiving vessel.

Move the pipette tip into the receiving vessel and release the liquid.

Once the liquid is drained from the pipette, touch the tip to the inside wall of the receiving vessel to ensure any drops of the liquid adhering to the pipette tip are deposited in the receiving vessel. There may be some liquid remaining in the tip of the pipette. The pipette is calibrated to take this into account; do not force liquid out of the pipette.


### Using a Micropipette


When using enzyme kits, or if an assay requires very small amounts of reagent, the micropipette is the tool for the job. The following is a general guide to using micropipettes:

Make sure the micropipette is the right one for the job (that its volume range encompasses the volume to be transferred). The volume range will be on the top of the micropipette. Usually, tips are color-coded to match the micropipette you are using.

Look on the window of the micropipette to see what volume it is set to. The highest number possible will represent the highest volume the micropipette is capable of dispensing (see Figure 5).

Adjust the numbers in the window to the volume to be transferred. This will be accomplished slightly differently depending upon the micropipette. For most micropipettes, unlock the volume determining knob and then twist the knob until the window shows the volume you would like to transfer.

Relock the volume determining knob.

Insert a fresh tip securely onto the micropipette.

Grasp the pipette with thumb on the pump at the top. Notice that if depressed the knob will stop, but when pressed a little harder the knob will continue to depress until it reaches a second stop.

Depress the knob to the first stop.

Submerge the tip into the liquid. Be sure to be far enough below the surface so that the tip will remain submerged while withdrawing liquid, but not so far below the surface that air pressure could force excess liquid into the tip. Avoid the sides and bottom of the container.

Slowly release the pressure on the knob. Ensure there are no air bubbles in the tip. The more viscous the liquid the slower the knob must be released to avoid bubbles.

Remove the tip from the liquid. Touch the tip to the side of the container to ensure that no liquid is on the outside of the tip. If needed, wipe the tip with a lab tissue to remove any excess liquid.

Touch the tip to the side of the receiving container.

Slowly depress the knob. Continue to depress until the second stop is reached.

Do not worry if there is liquid remaining in the tip. This has been accounted for in the calibration of the device. Remove the tip from the receiving vessel before releasing the pressure on the knob to avoid re-sucking liquid back up into the tip.

Dispose of the tip.


### Using a Burette


Burettes are used in titrations: when a measured amount of liquid is slowly added to your sample until an endpoint is reached (usually a change in color). Some simple considerations will ensure that no more error than is necessary is introduced during the titration.

Ensure the burette is clean. If liquid forms droplets in the burette or if there is visible debris, clean before using.

Rinse the burette with the titrant (liquid that will eventually fill the burette). Discard.

Fill the burette with the titrant. Open the stopcock (See Figure 6) and let some of the titrant run through. Discard.

Ensure there are no air bubbles in the burette above or below the stopcock. If there are, let more titrant run through and discard. If a bubble remains below the stopcock, but does not increase or decrease in size after repeated openings of the stopcock, it is safe to proceed.

Before titrating, be sure to record the starting volume in the burette. Avoid parallax errors and record from the bottom of the meniscus (Figure 7).

Titrate by opening and closing the stopcock as needed. When the stopcock is perpendicular to the burette it is closed and no titrant will flow. When the stopcock is parallel to the burette, it is open and titrant will flow. Positions between parallel and perpendicular will deliver titrant at various flow rates. In order to deliver the smallest amount of titrant possible, quickly turn the stopcock through 180° beginning and ending in the perpendicular position.

When the titration is completed, be sure to remove any drops of titrant that may be on the outside of the tip of the burette to avoid them falling into the next titration.

### Preparing Reagents


When preparing solutions of exact concentration or transferring exact volumes which are too large for pipettes, volumetric flasks (Figure 8) are the appropriate tool. Fill volumetric flasks to the fill line with the meniscus bottom touching the fill line, as with volumetric pipettes (Figure 4).

There are choices when it comes to preparing reagents for the lab. Primarily this means either purchasing premade solutions or making them as needed in-house. Premade solutions usually have guaranteed compositions and concentrations. They can be expensive, however, and their guaranteed composition may not extend past opening and/or may degrade over time. Making solutions can be much cheaper, and they can be made fresh for each use, however proper equipment and know-how are required to prepare these solutions.

Figure 8: Volumetric flasks

In either case, unless using unopened, premade, guaranteed, standardized solutions, the first step of most analyses will be to standardize your reagents. To standardize means to determine their actual concentration, not just what they are supposed to be (nominal concentration). For example, the method for titratable acidity calls for the sample to be titrated with 0.1 M sodium hydroxide (NaOH). If the NaOH was just made or is being used after being stored, it is important to confirm the concentration – ensuring no error was made in calculations or execution or that over time the NaOH has not degraded. The concentration of reagents can never be assumed to be correct; it must be standardized after being prepared or after storage.

Methods for standardization are included in this manual where needed. Without the standardization step, there is no way to know how reliable the results are. Note that there are instructions for preparing specific solutions found in at the end of this manual.


#### Preparing Solutions


Calculate the amount of the chemical and the volume of solvent (usually water) needed to create the required concentration.

Ensure the volumetric flask is clean. If liquid forms droplets in the flask or if there is visible debris, clean the flask before using.

Weigh out the required amount of chemical.

Ensure the deionized water (or solvent) you will be using is at the calibration temperature for the volumetric flask (usually 20 °C).

Add enough solvent to fill the volumetric flask about 1/3 full.

Using a funnel, add the chemical to the flask. Use a squirt bottle full of solvent to rinse any residue from the funnel or sides of the flask’s neck.

Stir the flask until the chemical has been completely dissolved.

Add enough solvent to the flask to bring the fill level into the neck. Use the squirt bottle to add solvent until the bottom of the meniscus sits on top of the fill line.


### Volumetric transfers


When the method calls for the contents of a volumetric flask to be transferred “volumetrically” use the following method:

Pour the contents of the volumetric flask into the receiving container.

Rinse the flask with deionized water and empty into the receiving container.

Rinse the flask and deposit in the receiving container two more times.


### Record Keeping


Record keeping is important for production, legal, and tax-related reasons. For the purposes of laboratory analysis, very specific records must be kept. Some things to consider when setting up records for lab testing:


#### METHOD PROTOCOLS


It is important to have a copy of the methods that are used. These may vary from lab to lab and it is important to have a record of the exact method used.


#### VALIDATION


Keeping a record of the validation is important so there is a record of the accuracy and precision of the method. As well, when re-validating after changing reagents or equipment, a comparison of accuracy and precision is possible. Having new employees re-validate methods is a great training tool as well.


#### Reagent Records


Record the lot number, expiry date, and when reagents were opened or made. If results start changing in an unexpected way, having these records allows tracing of how old reagents are or if different lots of chemicals are producing different results.


#### Test Results


It is important not only to record final results, but also the raw data that has been used to generate those results. This allows for re-calculation if later results don’t agree.
