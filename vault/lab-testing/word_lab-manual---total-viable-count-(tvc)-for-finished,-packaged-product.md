# word_lab manual - Total Viable Count (TVC) for Finished, Packaged Product

> Source: Cider Institute of North America course materials

## Total Viable Count (TVC) for Finished, Packaged Product


Note: All of the following must be completed under aseptic conditions.


### Equipment


Filtration equipment which has been sterilized (autoclaved)

Sterile 0.2 micron filter

Vacuum pump or hose connection

General growth media (such as YPD or Apple Rogosa)

Incubation chamber

Parafilm wax

Equipment for aseptic technique

70% ethanol

Sterile water (autoclaved)


### Method


Within a zone of sterility, wipe the package around where it will be opened with 70% ethanol and let dry.

Open the cider container.

Pour the cider into the filtering apparatus and filter through the 0.2 micron filter.

Pour sterile water into the package and rinse entire package.

Put this rinsate water through the filter as well.

Rinse the container 2 more times with sterile water and pass rinsate through the filter.

Turn off filter apparatus.

Aseptically transfer the 0.2 micron filter onto the general growth media.

Allow a minute or two for the filter to adsorb onto the media.

Seal the media with parafilm wax and incubate at 30 degrees Celsius for at least 5 days.

Each day, check the media for growth.


### Considerations


Each colony that grows on the media represents one cell found within the cider

A single yeast or bacterial cell in the finished cider is capable of causing microbial instability in the product

No growth after 5 days does not mean there were zero viable cells present in the cider. It does mean that no cells were present that were capable of growing under the conditions of the test (media, time, temperature, humidity, oxygen level, etc.). Therefore, report no growth as “Less than one” (not zero)

Most often, large filamentous, hairy, or fuzzy growths represent contamination of the test and not organisms present in the cider

Yeast colonies will generally appear white to beige, somewhat shiny, round, and of moderate size

Bacterial colonies may vary in appearance but generally will be noticeably smaller (pin point)


# Reagent recipes


Safety first!

Before using any chemical, consult its safety data sheet for important safety information about its use and storage.

Always add acids or bases to water, never the other way around. Adding water to acids and bases generates lots of heat and could even cause an explosion. Add acids and bases to water slowly, preferably in an ice bath to dissipate heat.

Don’t pour chemicals directly from their original containers. Find a beaker that is sized correctly for the amount you require and pour first into this beaker. Don’t return unused chemicals to the original containers. This prevents contamination.

Before using any chemical have a plan for its safe disposal. Consult with your local authorities about proper hazardous waste disposal.

#### 0.01 N Iodine


Dilute as appropriate from standard solution (purchased).


#### 1 % Methylene blue indicator


Dissolve 5 g Methylene blue in 500 mL deionized water.


#### 0.1% Methylene blue indicator


Dissolve 0.5 g Methylene blue in 500 mL deionized water.


#### Mixed indicator solution


Dissolve 0.1 g Methyl red and 0.05 g Methylene blue into approx. 100 mL of 50% ethanol.


#### Phenolphthalein


Dissolve 1 g solid Phenolphthalein in approx. 70 mL of 95% ethanol. Dilute to 100 mL with deionized water.


#### 0.2 M Phosphate buffer pH 8.0


Dissolve 1.27 grams of monobasic potassium phosphate and 50.81 g of dibasic potassium phosphate in 1 L of deionized water.


#### 25% Phosphoric acid


Add 294 mL of concentrated Phosphoric acid to approx. 700 mL of cold water in a 1 L volumetric flask. Once cooled, dilute to volume.


#### 0.05 M Potassium Hydrogen Phthalate (KHP)


Dissolve 1.021 g KHP volumetrically in 100 mL water (record actual weight to 3 decimal places and determine exact concentration).


#### 0.1 N Sodium Hydroxide


Dissolve 4 g of Sodium hydroxide in 1 L deionized water or dilute from purchased solution.


#### 0.01 N Sodium hydroxide


Dissolve 0.4 g of NaOH pellets in 1 L deionised water or dilute from purchased solution.


#### 1.55 N Sodium thiosulphate solution


Add approx. 50 ml of dH2O to a 100 mL volumetric flask. Dissolve 245 mg sodium thiosulphate in the water. Add a pinch of sodium bicarbonate and dilute to volume.


#### 1% Potato starch indicator


Add 10 g of potato starch to 1 L of water. Heat to boiling while stirring until dissolved.


#### 25% Sulfuric acid


Dilute 250 mL concentrated Sulfuric acid in 750 mL H20

Caution: extremely exothermic reaction…add acid slowly and allow heat to dissipate


# Glossary


°Brix or Degrees Brix: A measurement of density that is used to determine soluble solids in juice or to monitor the progress of fermentations. In solutions with no alcohol, degrees Brix represents grams of soluble solids per 100 g of solution. Once alcohol is present the relationship between degrees Brix and dissolved solids no longer holds.

Accuracy: How close a measured result is to the "true" value. The accuracy of a particular measurement can never be known, instead we measure the accuracy of the method.

Analysis: A test or investigation of a particular parameter of your cider. Usually you are trying to find the amount or concentration of a chemical, such as ethanol, in your cider when doing an analysis.

Analyte: A chemical that is the focus of an analytical test. Ex. ethanol or acetic acid.

Aseptic: Aseptic techniques are those that are done in sterile conditions to prevent contamination by microbes in the environment.

Assay: Synonym for test.

Atomic absorption spectrum: Instruments that measure the AA spectrum are usually used to determine the amount of particular elements, such as iron and calcium, that are present in a sample.

Bound Sulphur Dioxide: That portion of the Sulphur Dioxide present in juice or cider that is bound to other chemicals. Sulphur Dioxide will bind to acealdehyde, sugars, and phenols. Bound Sulphur Dioxide does not participate in anti-microbial properties of Sulphur Dioxide.

Buffer: A solution that is resistant to changes in pH. Buffers are reguarily used to calibrate pH meters due to their stable pH.

Burette: A volumetric piece of lab equipment that is capable of delivering a measured amount of reagent to a receiving vessel. Burettes are most often used in titrations, where small, measured amounts of reagents are slowly added to a beaker until the endpoint of a reaction with the contents of the beaker occurs.

Calibrate: the action of changing or modifying measuring equipment to increase it's accuracy. Most calibration is done once by the manufacturer of the equipment. One notable exception is the pH meter that must be calibrated every day.

Coefficient of Variation: A measurement of the amount of agreement within several measurements of the same sample. Calculated from the standard deviation and the mean. The Coefficient of variation compares the size of the variation to the size of the measurement.

Colony: A group of cells that grow on a media plate in microbial assays. Colonies, unlike single cells, are visible to the naked eye. Colonies of different organisms look different and their appearance can sometimes be used to identfy the microbe.

Critical Action Points: Points in the production process where your cider must meet certain conditions in order to ensure quality or safety. These may be association with a HACCP (Hazard Assessment Critical Control Points) program or more generally part of a Quality Assurance process.

Deionized Water (also dH2O): Water that has had all minerals removed. Usually this is accomplished by distillation. Becuase of this, sometimes dH2O will be called distilled water. All water that is used in analytical methods must be dionized.

Dextrose: Glucose molecules come in two different forms (sometimes colloquially called left-handed and right-handed), dextrose and levulose. For some reactions Glucose of only the dextrose form is prefered.

Diaphragm (microscope): The aperture below the stage that is opened or closed to allow more or less light through the sample.

Dissociation (acids): When hydrogen ions are attached to the acid molecule they are associated. Dissociated hydrogen ions are no longer attached to the acid molecule and instead, float free in the juice or cider.

Distillate: What is collected when a juice or cider sample is distilled (boiled into a vapour and then re-condensed back into a liquid).

Endpoint: The end of a titration method. When all the reagent in the beaker has been consumed in reaction with the reagent being slowly delivered from the burette, the excess reagent coming from the burette will react with an indicator chemical in the beaker, usually produing a noticible color change.

Free Sulphur Dioxide: The portion of Sulphur Dioxide in juice or cider that is not bound to any other chemicals. Only Free Sulphur Dioxide contributes to the formation of Molecular Sulphur Dioxide.

Gas chromatography: An instrumental method that separates all the chemical constituents of a sample for quantification. Gas chromatography can detect very small amounts of analytes and is routinely used for the determination of alcohol by volume.

Graduated glassware: Equipment in the lab that are used for holding liquids during analysis. They have volume measurements printed on the glass, but these are only used to estimate the amount of liquid. These are never used to measure samples or chemicals for use in analytical methods.

Gross errors: Large, obvious errors. These include adding too much chemicals to a reaction, or spilling the sample before measuring, or adding the wrong chemical.

Haemocytometer: A piece of equipment used, in conjunction with a microscope, to count the number of cells present in a sample.

High pressure liquid chromatography: A type of chromatography that can separate and measure individual chemicals in juice and cider. Most often LC methods are used to measure specific acids or sugars in juice or cider.

Hydrometry or Hydrometer: Measuring the density of a sample to determine a specific characteristic of juice or cider. A hydrometer is a precision piece of equipment that will float in a sample of juice or cider.

Indicator: A chemical that is added to a titration that reacts with the titrant once all of the analyte is consumed in the titration. Usually indicators change color when they react with the titrant.

Interferents: Conditions or chemicals that compromise the accuracy of a method. Interfering conditions are usually well known and measures to mediate the interferents are usually included in the method.

Inverted or Inversion: The process of converting Sucrose into Glucose and Fructose.

Malic acid: The main fixed acid in apples, juice, and cider. It is a weak organic acid.

Media: (singular: medium). A substrate for encouraging the growth of microbes in various microbiological tests. There are general media such as Yeast Peptone Digest and Apple Rogosa that promote the growth of all microorganisms and selective media such as Lysine Agar that allow for the growth of only specifc groups of microorganisms.

Meniscus: when liquid is in a closed cylinder, the liquid will adhere to the glass of the sides of the cylinder. The liquid in the center of the cylinder has no side to adhere to and so is pulled by gravity lower than the liquid on the sides. This creates a characteristic "u" shape. When taking measurements of liquids in a cylinder (burette, pipette, etc.), ensure you take the measurement at the bottom of the "u" (not at the sides that are attached to the glass).

Micropipette: An instrument that delivers a very precise and small amount of liquid. Micropipettes may be used for volumes as large as 5 mL, but most often are used for volumes below 1 mL.

Molarity: The concentration of a solution expressed as moles per litre. One mole is equal to 6.02 x 10^23 molecules.

Mole: A measure of the amount of a substance.  1 mole of a chemical is 6.02 x 10^23 molecules of the substance.

Molecular Sulphur Dioxide: The portion of Sulphur Dioxide in juice or cider that is in a form that acts as an anti-microbial. Molecular Sulphur Dioxide concentration is dependent upon the Free Sulphur Dioxide concentration and the pH of the juice or cider. To be effective as an anti-microbial agent Molecular Sulphur Dioxide must be between 0.5 and 0.8 mg/L.

Near infrared detectors: Infrared-based instrumental analysis measures the amount of absorbance of infrared light of a sample and mathematically estimates the amount of analyte(s) that are present in the sample.

Nominal Concentration: The supposed or labelled concentration of a solution or reagent. The actual concentration must be determined by standardization.

Normality or Normal (N): A measure of the concentration of reactive units in a solution. 1 N (One Normal) indicates that there are 1 mole of reactive units per litre. For chemicals with only one reactive unit, Normality = Molarity. For chemicals with more than one reactive unit, Normality is a multiple of the Molarity. For example, malic acid is a di-acid and therefore has two reactive units (two acid parts to the molecule). 1 M of malic acid (1 mole/litre) is equal to 2 N of malic acid (2 moles of reactive units/litre).

Objective Lens (microscope): The movable lenses of various magnifications found just above the stage. Contrasted with the Ocular lens which is the lens that is part of the eyepiece of the microscope.

Ocular Lens (microscope): The lenses found in the eyepiece of the microscope. Contrasted with the objective lenses which are found on the movable carrousel just above the stage.

Organic acids: Acids present in juice and cider that have carbon atoms in their structure. The most common organic acid in apple juice and cider is malic acid. In pears, citric acid is usually the most common organic acid.

Parallax Errors: Errors in measurement due to not being eye-level with the measuring device.

pH: A measure of the strength of the acids present in juice or cider as determined by the concentration of free hydrogen ions. pH is important for determining the microbial stability of juice or cider and the efficacy of sulphur dioxide as an anti-microbial agent.

Pipette: a volumetric piece of equipment that allows for the precise measurement and transfer of exact volumes of reagents.

Precision: How closely two measurements of the same sample agree with each other. This is also related to how fine the measurement scale is.

Quality Assurance (QA): All of the thiings that you do in your day to day operation that are concerned with producing a quality product.

Quality Control (QC): Specific testing or analysis that is done to make sure that Quality Assurance programs are working.

Random errors: Errors that are due to the chaotic nature of the universe. Controling the influence of light, vibration, air flow, temperature fluctuations and other environmental factors can help to reduce random error.

Reagents: The chemicals that are involved in a chemical reaction.

Recovery: A measure of how much of a known analyte a method is able to measure. Recoveries of between 95 and 105% are usually considered acceptable.

Reducing sugars: Those sugars that are able to participate in redox reactions and are thus detectable using redox-based titrations. Notably sucrose is NOT a reducing sugar. Reducing sugar is sometimes abbreviated RS, but should not be confused with the more colloquial term Residual Sugar.

Reduction-oxidation also Redox: Reactions that involve the transfer of electrons from one chemical to another. One of the chemicals gains electrons (is reduced) and one donates electrons (is oxidized).

Refractometry or Refractometer: Measuring the refractive index of a sample to determine characteristics of your juice or cider. Most often a refractometer is used to determine soluble solids in juice. Refractometry is not possible in cider that contains alcohol.

Soluble solids: All of the chemicals that are dissolved in juice or cider. About 85 to 95% of soluble solids in juice are sugars.

Solution: A liquid that is made up of a solvent (usually water) and a solute - something that is dissolved in the solvent. Solutions can have multiple solutes.

Solvent: A liquid, usually water, that a chemical is dissolved within to make a reagent for use in analyses.

Specific gravity: A measure of density that is used to determine the soluble solids in juice and to montor the progress of fermentations. It is a measure of how dense the juice or cider is as compared to water. For specific gravity measurements above 1.000, the juice or cider is more dense than water. For measurements below 1.000, the juice or cider is less dense than water.

Spectrophotometer: An instrument that can determine how much light is absorbed at specific wavelengths for your sample. This can be used in conjunction with enzyme kits to determine the concentration of a large range of analytes in juice and cider.

Stage (microscope): The platform where slides are placed below the lenses to be viewed.

Standard deviation: A mathematical expression of how well repeated measurements of the same sample agree with each other. It is calculated based upon how far each measurement is from the average of all measurements.

Standardize: Measure the exact concentration of a reagent before using them in analyses.

Stopcock: the device at the bottom of a burette that is spun to allow titrant to flow from the burette into the beaker below.

Systemic errors: Also called biases. These are errors due to the method itself (the equipment, environment, chemicals etc.) that create a stable error, usually in one direction (postive bias or negative bias). Systemic errors can only be removed by changing your method, equipment, or reagents.

Titrant: The reagent that is filled into a burette and slowly delivered during a titration test.

Titratable acidity (TA): A measure of all the acidic hydrogen ions that can be neutralized with a basic chemical. This comprises all acids in the cider, both fixed (malic acid, citric acid, etc.) and volatile (acetic acid, lactic acid etc.). TA is expressed in gram/Litre of malic acid. TA is an indication of the acidic sensory profile of a juice or cider.

Titration: An analysis technique whereby a small, measured amount of one reagent is slowly added to another reagent until an endpoint is reached. Usually titrations are based upon acid/base or redox reactions.

Total Cell Count: The concentration of cells in a sample. This type of count does not distinguished between cells that are viable and non-viable (alive or dead).

Total Sulphur Dioxide: The combined concentrations of both Bound Sulphur Dioxide and Free Sulphur Dioxide in juice and cider.

Validation: The process where by an analytical method is evaluated for its accuracy and precision. Validation is done once, when the method is being set up in the cidery. Methods need to be re-validated when reagents, equipment, or the environment changes. New technicians should validate methods for themselves to ensure they are capable of the level of accuracy and precision that the method is validated for.

Viable Cell Count: A measurement of the concentration of cells that are capable of being alive in a sample. The cell count is usually expressed as cells per mL.

Volumetric Equipment: Glassware that is guaranteed to deliver a specific and precise amount of liquid. Volumetric glassware, such as volumetric flasks and pipettes are required for most wet chemistry methods.

Volumetric Flask: A piece of equipment that, when filled to a marked line, contains an exact amount of liquid. Used most often for the preparation of reagents, but can also be used to measure volumes and do volumetric transfers.
