# word_lab manual - Safety

> Source: Cider Institute of North America course materials

## Safety


You know what they say: “Safety first”. Truly, safety is the first thing to consider when undertaking lab analyses in your cider. Safety starts with the physical environment that you intend to use for analysis of your cider. The area should be free of obstructions that could be trip hazards, table-tops should be free of items that may topple or otherwise interfere with your work, and only trained personnel should be allowed in the area (and that includes pets!).

While cider is generally a very safe product to work with, some of the reagents needed for cider analysis could cause sickness, injury, or could even be fatal. Analysis methods are built on a foundation of general lab safety principles and best practices including:

Ensure that there is enough uncluttered space for the work that needs to be done.

All equipment, storage containers, and glassware should be in good repair.

Have the appropriate personal protective equipment available for the work to be done. Usually this means lab coat, eye protection, and gloves.

Be prepared for spills and accidents. Have a spill kit, eyewash station, and a posted list of emergency numbers.

If working with flames, ensure there is no loose clothing or hair.

Don’t eat, drink, smoke, or apply makeup in the lab space.

Don’t work from large containers of reagents. When needed, pour a small amount into a smaller container. This prevents contamination and avoids large spills.

Have a plan for disposing of waste. Nothing should ever be put down the drain; not only because of potential harm to organisms in the environment (including humans), but also many reagents are corrosive and will destroy your pipes. Waste containers should be secure, not over-filled, and disposed of by a licensed waste disposal company.

One of the most important tools for lab safety are the Safety Data Sheets (SDS). These will be supplied with every purchase (either paper or online). SDS’s list the potential harms of the product, the appropriate safety protocols, safe disposal, and what to do in case of emergency. SDS’s are part of a larger, international safety protocol, the Globally Harmonized System (GHS). Further, different jurisdictions may have specific safety protocols for the handling and storage of chemicals. Be sure of all the safety and legal requirements before bringing any chemical into the cidery.
