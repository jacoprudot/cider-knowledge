## Soluble Solids By Hydrometrey


Methods that measure total soluble solids give a good indication of the sugar content in apple juice, as sugars can represent over 95% of the total soluble solids. Other components of the soluble solids in apple juice include acids, the salts of acids, pectin, tannins, and pigments. Generally, in North America, beer producers use specific gravity to measure soluble solids while wine producers use °Brix°. Cider producers use both. Both measurements are based upon the density of the juice (mass divided by volume) and thus are really just different units for measuring the same thing.

Specific gravity is a unitless ratio of the density of the juice or cider compared to the density of water. Juice has a high concentration of sugar, which is more dense than water, so the specific gravity will be above 1.000. Cider contains alcohol, which is less dense than water, so the specific gravity will be close to, or below, 1.000.

Soluble solids can be measured by hydrometry or refractometry. Refractometry measures the refractive index of a solution, which can be used to determine the soluble solids content. However, the refractometer is very sensitive to any solid particles or gas bubbles in the sample and becomes completely unreliable in the presence of alcohol. Refractometry can be a handy tool in the orchard to estimate sugar levels. However, in the cidery, soluble solids are almost exclusively measured using hydrometry.

Hydrometry is based on the principle that an object will displace a volume of liquid equivalent to its weight. Eureka! (…anyone?) The volume displaced by the object is inversely proportional to the density of the liquid, so a solution of high density will show less displacement than one of lower density for the same object. More plainly: the denser the juice, the higher the hydrometer will float in it.


### Interferents


Hydrometer readings are based upon the density of solutions, which changes in response to temperature. To avoid inconsistencies, hydrometers are calibrated to a specific temperature. Samples must be brought to the calibration temperature, or a temperature correction must be applied to the reading. A rough calculation for this correction is found in Tables 4 and 5.

Ethanol is less dense than water and can depress hydrometer readings (after fermentation is complete, cider may have a negative °Brix and may be below 1.000 specific gravity). Ethanol also inflates refractive index readings. The interference is so severe that refractometers are not appropriate for use after fermentation commences. For hydrometry the interference means that once fermentation begins, the relationship between density and amount of sugar no longer holds.

Bubbles of gas, especially carbon dioxide (CO2) formed during fermentation may adhere to the sides of the hydrometer during measurement and artificially increase the buoyancy of the hydrometer. Once the hydrometer is submerged in the sample, it should be spun to remove any bubbles that may be adhering before taking a reading. If the fermentation is particularly active, accurate measurement may be impossible.


### Equipment


°Brix or specific gravity hydrometer

250 mL glass or clear plastic cylinder (either graduated or not graduated)

20°C water bath or incubator

Thermometer


### Method


Fill a 250 mL cylinder with sample.

Measure the temperature of the sample. If the calibrated temperature of the hydrometer differs from the temperature of the sample, either adjust the temperature of the sample (preferable) or make temperature corrections to the reading (see Tables 4 and 5).

Gently place the hydrometer in the cylinder and spin the hydrometer to remove any adhering bubbles.

Take the reading at the bottom of the meniscus once a constant flotation level is reached.
