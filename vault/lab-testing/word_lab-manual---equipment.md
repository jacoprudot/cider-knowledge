# word_lab manual - Equipment

## Equipment


What equipment do you need in a cider lab? This can only ever be a rhetorical question because the answer varies with each production facility. Some will have nothing and send all their samples away for testing, while some will have expensive instruments for advanced analysis. In reality, most cideries fall somewhere in between these two extremes. The answer to the question, though, must be informed by each cidermaking practice. What analyses are needed to monitor critical action points? For these critical tests, more precise and accurate equipment will be worth the investment.

On the other hand, are there routine tests performed most often? It might be better to invest in equipment that can provide faster analysis, even if this means compromising some accuracy or precision. How big or small is the tolerance for error? How much in-house knowledge is there? Even asking all the right questions is beyond the scope of this manual. Each cidery and cidermaker will have to answer these questions for themselves. There are, however, some things to keep in mind when discussing equipment for analysis.


### Types of Analyses


When thinking about analytical tests, most people think about wet chemistry. Wet chemistry is a colloquial term that describes analyses that are completed on lab benches with glassware and liquid solutions – hence “wet”. There are, however, other ways to analyze cider, such as using biochemical tests or advanced instrumental analyses. Each of these methods have different pros and cons. There are many factors to consider, such as price, speed, accuracy, required technical knowledge, and space requirements.

Wet chemistry is the basic, traditional way of measuring components of cider and is used in many different applications. These methods tend to be fairly cheap, require little space, and are usually fast. On the other hand, these methods usually require a certain level of in-house knowledge and skill in order to produce reliable results.

Biochemical analyses use small amounts of sample and biological enzymes to assess the amount of a particular parameter in cider. These methods require a spectrophotometer capable of detecting absorption in visible and ultraviolet wavelengths. A good spectrophotometer can be an investment piece in your lab, usually running in the thousands of dollars. Once you have a spectrophotometer, however, you can use enzyme kits to analyze for almost any component of cider you would be interested in characterizing, including acetic acid, acetaldehyde, malic acid, lactic acid, and many more. These kits can be extremely precise and accurate, however they do require superior micropipetting skills. Beyond biochemical assays, the spectrophotometer can also be used to characterize the color of cider and can be used in conjunction with non-enzymatic assays, such as the Folin-Ciocalteu method for measuring phenolics, or the NOPA assay for Yeast Assimilable Nitrogen.

The most expensive and, generally, most accurate and precise methods for analyzing cider are instrumental methods such as gas chromatography, near infrared detectors, or atomic absorption spectrum analyzers. Many of these pieces of equipment require 5 and 6-figure investments and can only be used by highly trained technicians. Instrumental analysis can be very selective (no other substances interfere with detection) and sensitive (able to detect small amounts). For example, high pressure liquid chromatography can be used to measure not only the TA of your cider, but can also separate and quantify the different acids present, such as malic, citric, and quinic acids. Most cider producers will only have access to these methods through commercial or academic laboratories, however some instruments in the marketplace are becoming affordable for medium to large sized producers.

Microbiological methods are a bit different than those for chemical methods in that they involve live organisms and require aseptic technique. Media plating, samples, and tests must all be performed in sterile conditions to prevent contamination. The specific requirements for microbiological testing will be discussed in a later section devoted to microbiological assays.

One final word about choosing methods. Think back to the two questions that are most important when doing analyses: “What action will I take based upon this result,” and, “How much do I trust this result?” It can be important to remember those two questions when evaluating what types of testing you will use. One final category of testing methods are the all-in-one, tabletop, automatic testing devices. These are usually push-button devices that display a digital result. Some of these are great, and some are worthless. Be cautious when evaluating if one of these mini-lab devices is the right solution. Without some sense of the accuracy and precision of these units, it may be difficult to know how much you can trust the results. Ask to see validation numbers for the device: What is the accuracy? What is the precision?

Keep in mind, as well, with no way to standardize reagents, the devices rely upon fresh, non-expired chemicals that usually have to be purchased from the device’s manufacturer. For basic wet chemistry, many times chemicals past their expiry dates can be used, provided they are standardized first. With all-in-one units, chemicals that have expired should not be used.


### Glassware


The first thing to consider when thinking about how to stock your cidery lab is the  difference between volumetric and graduated glassware. Volumetric equipment is guaranteed by the manufacturer to hold the stated volume at the calibrated temperature within a stated margin of error. Volumetric equipment includes volumetric pipettes and volumetric flasks; for smaller volumes this also includes micropipettes (Figure 2).

Graduated glassware, such as graduated cylinders, beakers, and flasks have marks indicating volume. These are not accurate or suitable for measuring volumes for analysis, but they are good for measuring approximate volumes (Figure 3).

Figure 3: Graduated glassware.

Glassware must be kept clean. Any dirt or oil on the surface of the glass will cause inaccurate measurements. Water wets clean glassware evenly. Therefore, if there are any droplets of water adhering to the inside of your glassware, the glass is dirty and needs to be cleaned. There are specific detergents made for lab glassware. When cleaning, soak the glassware in a warm solution of the detergent and gently scrub with cleaning brushes. Rinse at least three times with deionized water. Deionized water (dH2O) is water that has had all of the dissolved minerals and other dissolved solids removed – usually through distillation. Either distill your own or purchase distilled water (usually found in drug stores). Never dry glassware with compressed air

or blowing as this will redeposit soil and grease on the glass. If the glass is not dry and needs to be used right away, rinse the glass with the solution that will be used and then discard. Do this three times.
